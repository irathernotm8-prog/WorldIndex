# Pantheon

*The age before Valkorath*

Pantheon

## Chapter 1: Ashes of the Old World

The sky over Elarion was stained with the bruised colors of war. Where once golden rays fell through verdant canopies and across marble cities, now smoke choked the heavens and the gods had turned their gaze inward--toward power, pride, and paranoia.

Long ago, the gods had walked among mortals as guardians. Their hands shaped rivers and raised mountains. Their voices stirred thunder and soothed flame. But time, as ever, proved corrosive. Reverence twisted into resentment, and even the divine grew wary of their creations. Among the mortals rose a man named Manus--brilliant, ambitious, and doomed.

Manus did what no mortal before him dared: he killed a god.

None remember which god fell first, only that it bled black and screamed. In slaying it, Manus cursed himself, and in time, his soul would fracture and fester. But in that moment, the balance cracked.

The gods reacted not with unity, but with fear.

At the center of the divine council, Gavinus, son of the Lightbearer Valdre, called for the extinguishing of mortal will. "They grow bold," he warned, "infected with belief in their own worth." But others hesitated. Chief among them was Ordovis, once a war-god of steel, now a guardian of green. He had grown weary of bloodshed. He warned, "Burn the roots, and you doom the forest."

Not all gods were present.

Serosh, shunned by his kin, watched from afar, crimson eyes glowing beneath his hood. His staff pulsed with unspoken knowledge. Cast out for views too grim even for the divine, he whispered prophecies to the winds that no one wished to hear.

And then, in the far reaches of the realm, a flash of light cracked the sky. Not fire. Not divine wrath. Something… else. From the void stepped Andromedus, blue-skinned and silent, drawn to Elarion not to conquer but to observe. None knew yet why he came. None but Manus.

Beneath the shattered peaks of the south, Renfry, warlord of flame, gathered his templars. From the cold marshes, Orion, god of blight, raised the dead in defiance of all order. And in the north, Desaad's legions marched, cloaked in firelight, whispering of a reckoning to come.

The gods bickered. The mortals sharpened blades. And far beyond all comprehension, the Dark One stirred.

A storm was coming.

One that would end the age of gods.

And begin the rise of something else.

## Chapter 2: Sparks Beneath the Ash

The realm of Elarion was vast, wild, and wounded.

In the early years, mortals lived beneath the gaze of the gods--praying, fearing, and obeying. But now? The skies grew quieter. The temples colder. And faith began to fracture.

Across the lands, fractured city-states and isolated strongholds clung to power, ruled by warlords who masqueraded as kings and priests who spoke in riddles. None ruled the realm--not truly. And none could explain why the old gods no longer answered.

But in the mountains of the north, a voice did speak.

It whispered to a man named Manus--a warlord scarred by countless battles and haunted by loss. He had once wept for a child the gods would not save. Now he sought a blade that would pierce divinity itself. Manus, the first mortal to slay a god, would become something else entirely. Something cursed.

In the east, cloaked in crimson and crowned in gold, the false king Lucious made secret pacts with dark beings--chief among them, the silent god Drexl, who offered no miracles, only power. At Lucious's side stood Frenry, god of flame and leader of the Knights Templar of Deneath. To follow Frenry was to believe that fire was the truest form of faith.

In the south, where the forests still breathed with ancient life, Ordovis awoke. Once a mortal knight, now bound to the will of nature itself, he became a guardian of the green--a god not of temples, but of balance. He spoke little, but every step he took sowed life into the land beneath him.

In the west, a figure cloaked in mystery and shadow simply watched. No name, no temple. Only rumors: The Dark One. A presence felt, not seen. Feared, not followed.

Above it all, the gods still quarreled.

Serosh, adorned in crimson eyes and hidden knowledge, stood alone--shunned by the others for speaking of a great unraveling. He warned of a world reborn in mortal flame. The others called him mad. But mortals began to listen.

Orion, the god of blight, walked among dead trees, gathering a host of disease and decay. Desaad, of the charred desert winds, whispered into the dreams of tyrants. And Reign, once mortal, now Beast God, roamed the wilds--half-man, half-divine, rage made flesh.

And then, the heavens cracked.

From beyond Elarion, a bolt of impossible lightning struck the highlands--and through it came Thor, laughing and bloodthirsty, hammer in hand. He came not to save Elarion, but to test it. He would know if their gods were worthy--or break them one by one.

The gods stirred.

The mortals watched.

And something ancient--something blue and not of this world--began to awaken in a place beyond time. Andromedus.

The age of silence was over.

## Chapter 3: The Blade That Should Not Be Drawn

The mountains of Blackreach were older than the gods themselves--jagged bones of the earth that scraped the sky and swallowed men whole. It was there that Manus vanished, and there that he returned, changed.

He had not gone in search of power. Not at first. He had gone seeking answers--why the gods turned away from mankind, why his son had died with no blessing, no miracle, no whisper from the heavens. All Manus had received was silence. So he walked into the black.

And the black answered.

Deep beneath Blackreach, in a place no map named, he found it: a temple of ash and bone, half-swallowed by the earth. Its walls hummed. Its air tasted like memory. And in its center lay a sword--not forged, but grown. Its edge was obsidian, veined with dark gold. Its hilt pulsed faintly, like a dying heart.

“You are seen,” a voice had said--not aloud, but in his mind.

“You are chosen.”

When he gripped the sword, the darkness took hold.

Manus returned from the mountain weeks later, his eyes sunken, his armor scorched. He did not speak. But his blade did. In his wake came whispers, and where he walked, light withered. The old gods felt it--especially Gavinus, son of Valdre, the god of light. He declared Manus an abomination, a heretic, a traitor to the divine order.

But Manus did not care for their order. He had tasted divinity, and found it frail.

He would not worship gods.

He would replace them.

The first to fall was the mountain god Mytrax, a being of stone and flame. He descended upon Manus with fury, wielding earthquakes and molten wrath.

He died screaming.

The blade had a name, though none dared speak it aloud. It fed on godflesh, grew with each soul it consumed. And Manus? He stopped being just a man. His skin darkened, smoke trailing from his steps. His voice became distant, echoing as though spoken through a canyon. Mortals began to whisper the name that would one day haunt every prayer:

“The Prime Evil.”

But not all saw Manus as villain. In the fractured lowlands, rebels and outcasts flocked to his banner. Warriors who had buried children. Mothers who had burned in temples. Mercenaries who had seen too many "blessings" granted only to kings.

They called him Savior.

And so began the great fracture.

The gods turned on each other. Some, like Serosh and Ordovis, urged caution, warning that to strike Manus would only make him a martyr. Others, like Gavinus, burned with righteous fury and sought war.

And in the deep woods, beneath roots that remembered the First Dawn, a knight knelt before the green light of Ordovis and asked:

“If the gods die… who shapes the world?”

None had an answer.

## Chapter 4: The Band Beneath the Flame

The winds over the western ridges carried with them the scent of ash and steel. Once lush with cedar and song, the lands now bore the scars of celestial judgment. Temples were hollowed,

fields salted by fire, and statues of gods that once stood proud were shattered--by mortal hands.

Among the rubble walked Valerius.

Not a king. Not yet a symbol. Just a man with a sword--and a reason to raise it.

He had fought in the old campaigns, wearing the crest of the gods on his shield, leading divine-blessed armies into skirmishes against unbelievers. He had believed. He had knelt. He had served.

And then he saw what the gods did to those who doubted.

A village left burning for questioning tithes. A child struck by lightning for speaking Serosh's name aloud. The divine had become tyrants--and Valerius broke his blade across a holy altar and never looked back.

Now, he stood at the edge of a forgotten battlefield, surrounded by a small, strange fellowship.

Tiavoss, the Iron-Clad Warden, stood silently at his side. A warrior of unmatched strength, he had once sworn fealty to Gavinus himself, but now bore a scar across his chest where Gavinus had nearly burned him alive. Tiavoss never spoke of that day. But his eyes told the tale.

Eliegh, the Stone-Bound, knelt nearby, carving protective runes into the ground with his bare hands. Once a mortal man, Eliegh had been blessed--or cursed--by Ordovis, his flesh hardened like bark, his veins pulsing with living moss. Where Valerius brought fire, Eliegh brought rooted resolve.

Felough, youngest of the three, leaned on a curved blade crackling with stormlight. His sword, Stormbite, was said to be kin to the one that broke Desaad. Though loyal to Valerius, doubt clung to him like mist. He questioned the cause--not out of fear, but because he still remembered the gods as teachers, not tyrants.

They called themselves nothing.

They bore no banners.

But word of their defiance had spread like wildfire.

The cult of Father Godskin had placed a bounty on Valerius's head. Frenry's templars had begun scouring the eastern roads for signs of his passage. And high above, in a citadel lit only by judgment-fire, Drexl watched from behind a mask of iron.

Still, Valerius walked on.

He did not seek Manus. He did not worship him. He knew what the Prime Evil was becoming.

But he also knew this: the gods had failed.

And someone would have to build the world after they fell.

“Come,” he said, stepping over a collapsed statue of Valdre. “Let's give them something to fear.”

The firelight caught his sword just as a horn blew in the valley below. The knights of Deneath were near.

And the war for Elarion had begun.

## Chapter 5: When Men Defy Heaven

The valley below Stonegrave Pass was death waiting to happen.

Narrow, jagged, and shadowed by cliffs on both sides, it had once been a holy place where pilgrims came to lay offerings before the god Valdre. Now, it was the perfect place to kill a legend--or make one.

Valerius and his band stood at its edge.

Scouts had confirmed the rumors: Frenry's templars--elite warriors wreathed in flame, bound by blood and doctrine--were marching up the valley. Lucious had sent them not merely to kill Valerius, but to make an example of him.

Let the gods see what happens when mortals forget their place.

Valerius knelt beside a rock, carving a symbol into it with his blade: not of a god, but of a new world. No words. Just a rising sun.

Behind him, Tiavoss was readying a shield wall of scavenged plate and broken banners. Eliegh pressed his hands into the earth, awakening old roots to create a natural bulwark. Felough stood still, eyes on the horizon, his sword humming.

"They'll expect us to run," Felough muttered.

"Then let them learn," Valerius replied.

The horn blew.

The first wave of templars charged--men glowing with firelight, their blades burning with holy flame. They descended the valley like an avalanche.

And met stone, steel, and fury.

Tiavoss broke the front line with a shoulder charge that shook the cliffs. Eliegh called forth jagged roots to entangle enemy legs, turning the battlefield into a living trap. Felough moved like the storm itself--his sword arcing through flame, cutting through chants of false divinity.

And Valerius--he fought without prayer. Without blessing. Only purpose.

When the last templar fell, choking on blood and disbelief, Valerius looked to the heavens and said nothing.

The gods would hear his silence.

Meanwhile…

Far above Elarion, the divine halls of Aetherspire trembled--not from war, but from fear.

Gavinus, son of Valdre, paced furiously across the marbled floor. “They kill our chosen. They desecrate our temples. And still we do nothing?”

“No,” came the low voice of Drexl, emerging from a veil of crimson smoke. “We watch. We wait. We judge.”

“You always wait,” Gavinus spat. “Soon there will be nothing left to judge.”

Across the chamber, Serosh laughed from his shadowed perch. “Let them rise. Let them burn. The era of balance is over. Either we evolve… or we die.”

Drexl turned to him, slow and sharp. “You forfeited your voice the day you poisoned the mortal dreams.”

Serosh leaned forward, smiling. “And you forfeited yours the day you started listening to them.”

A deep rumble echoed through the chamber--ancient stone quaking.

In the center of the chamber, the Celestial Flame--a fire said to reflect the state of the world--flickered.

Then dimmed.

Then turned the color of blood.

A silence fell over the gods.

Something had shifted.

## Chapter 6: The Flame and the Echo

The sun never reached the floor of Hollowspire Wood. Its twisted trees knotted together like bones, their roots coiled as if hiding something beneath. Here, far from holy ground, far from mortal cities and divine towers, Valerius marched.

The battle at Stonegrave Pass had become more than victory. It had become rumor.

Tales spread faster than templars could burn them. A man had stood against the will of gods and lived. A man without blessing. A man without chains. Across scattered hamlets and ruined fortresses, whispers took shape.

And with those whispers came followers.

They arrived in silence. Former soldiers, farmers, even a one-eyed priest who burned his own scrolls and carried nothing but a rusted mace. They said nothing of faith. Only of purpose.

Valerius didn't want worship. He wanted witnesses. He wanted those who had seen what the gods had done and could still raise a sword.

As the warband passed deeper into the woods, Eliegh slowed near a gnarled trunk and touched the bark. It pulsed faintly beneath his hand.

"These trees are old," he murmured, voice like gravel in wind. "They remember something. Something… watching."

Felough scoffed. "More spirits? We've got enough to deal with--"

A flash of blue light ripped through the fog.

The trees bent as if bowing.

And standing in the clearing ahead, not ten paces from Valerius, was Andromedous.

No one spoke.

He did not walk. He hovered. Wrapped in robes of liquid fabric, skin faintly luminescent, his eyes held no pupils, only motion -- galaxies spinning in place. Lightning traced through the air around him like a nervous pulse.

Felough raised his blade. “God or ghost?”

Neither.

Andromedous tilted his head slightly, observing Valerius not like prey or threat… but puzzle.

Then he spoke, not aloud, but into their minds.

“This world is sick with gods.”

Valerius stepped forward. “And you are?”

“I am... the next step. I come not to rule. I come to remember.”

The wind stilled. Even the birds held their breath.

“You stand on the edge of erasure. Your blood carries two paths. One leads to a crown. The other leads to the end of all names.”

Eliegh moved beside Valerius. “This is madness.”

Andromedous turned slowly toward him.

“No, stone-bearer. Madness was what came before. I am clarity.”

Then he vanished.

Not in light, not in sound -- simply gone.

Valerius stared at the spot where the being had stood. His hands trembled. Not from fear. From recognition. He had felt that presence before--in his dreams, in the space between memory and silence.

It knew him.

And it knew what he would become.

Elsewhere…

In a crypt carved into the bones of the world, beneath a ruined temple once blessed by Valdre, Manus knelt before a mirror of obsidian.

He was no longer the man who had buried his child.

No longer even the man who had slain a god.

His skin cracked like cooled magma. His voice echoed before he spoke. The blade--his blade--rested against the stone, humming like a creature not yet fed.

Before him stood Breylar, his creation, his heir in mortal form.

Clad in red armor scorched from fire rituals, Breylar had been forged from pain--tortured, rebuilt, and honed like a weapon. The most powerful mortal to walk Elarion. Hidden until now.

“Is it time?” Breylar asked, voice hollow.

Manus nodded once.

"Valerius has begun to believe."

“And what will you do?”

“I will let him rise.”

Breylar clenched his fists. “You want him to kill the gods.”

“I want him to try.”

Above them, the earth trembled as something vast shifted.

The gods, for all their might, had not felt it yet.

But something older than them had begun to wake.

## Chapter 7: The Fracture Divine

The chambers of Aetherspire had not held them all in centuries.

Twelve thrones, arranged in a circle of carved starlight and mirrored stone, had long sat empty, collecting dust and silence. Only Valdre, the elder god, had remained seated through the ages--waiting, watching, wasting.

But now, the circle was full again.

Even the air shimmered with unease.

Gavinus, golden-eyed and burning, stood tall beside his father's throne, fists clenched in frustration. “How long will we whisper about mortals while they march on us?”

No one answered at first.

Then a chair shifted, and Radeem, the war god, leaned forward, resting one gauntleted hand on his curved blade. “Let them march,” he growled. “I've grown tired of waiting for a real fight.”

Baldri, cloaked in starlight and shadow, spoke from beneath his hood. “You may find that this war offers no victors. Only silence.”

“And what would you know of it?” Gavinus snapped. “You sleep while the world burns.”

“I dream of what comes after it burns,” Baldri murmured. “There is no throne at the end of this war. Only memory.”

Across from them, Orion said nothing, his pale, blight-marked form slouched against a throne of withered wood. Moss clung to his robes. Flies hovered near him, but never landed. He stared at the floor, eyes distant, as though still hearing the forest weep.

Drexl stepped forward.

He wore no crown. He never had to.

His red eyes scanned each god like a blade sweeping a battlefield. “Valerius is no threat alone. But the idea of him is. The gods are no longer believed--they are questioned. And that… is heresy.”

“Then end him,” said Ostheus, crackling with static. His armor sparked as he leaned back in his chair. “Strike him down like we did the others. Like we should've done to Manus before he became whatever he is now.”

From the far edge of the circle, a voice cut through the air like oil over fire.

Serosh.

Hooded. Calm. Smiling.

“You speak of ending men,” he said, “but have you asked yourselves why they began?”

The room grew cold.

Valdre finally stirred.

His voice was older than language. “Because we failed.”

Gavinus looked at him, stunned.

Valdre continued, eyes distant. “We were not made to rule. We were made to guide. But we grew hungry. Arrogant. Some among us tried to become kings. Others tried to become gods of gods. And now… they come with swords.”

Serosh stepped into the center of the circle. “You call me mad. You cast me out. And yet it is I who prepared for this moment. The fall of heaven is not a punishment--it is evolution.”

Drexl's hand twitched.

In an instant, the air warped, and Drexl was in front of Serosh, blade at his throat.

“You speak of evolution,” Drexl hissed. “But you spread rot.”

“And yet you fear me,” Serosh whispered.

“Not fear,” Drexl replied. “Judgment.”

Before the blade could fall, Valdre raised a hand.

The room shook with his will.

“Enough,” the elder god commanded.

Drexl withdrew. Serosh smiled.

Valdre turned his gaze upon the circle.

“We have delayed the inevitable. The mortals will rise. The gods will fall. And when the dust clears… only those who understand balance will remain.”

“Then what do we do?” asked Gavinus, voice low.

Valdre closed his eyes.

“We watch. We wait. And when the time comes… we choose who burns with the old world--and who stands to build the new.”

And far below the spire, in the bones of the world, the Dark One moved for the first time in an age.

No words.​ No warnings.​ Just a single step… forward.

## Chapter 8: The Weight of Banners

The wind on the Ridge of Darnan carried strange sounds--metal on stone, voices in prayer, wings without flight. Valerius stood at its edge, cloak snapping behind him, watching dust rise from the lowlands below.

The road ahead led toward Elysium Hollow, an ancient ruin swallowed by vines and prophecy. Eliegh claimed the ground there still hummed with the roots of Ordovis. If the band was to build something lasting--anything at all--they would need a place untouched by godly banners.

And yet, the weight of what they had become was beginning to crack the seams.

There were over sixty in their ranks now--no longer just a band, but a movement. Fighters, scouts, even defected priests and smiths. Some whispered titles behind Valerius's back: The Mortal Flame. The Breaker of Chains. He ignored them.

But Felough did not.

He sat sharpening Stormbite on a stone, staring at nothing.

“These people think you're a god,” he muttered.

Valerius didn't turn. “Then they're no better than the ones we're fighting.”

Felough scoffed. “You know what happens when someone becomes a symbol?”

“They stop being a man.”

“They stop being questioned.”

Valerius was quiet for a long time. Then: “Would you rather someone else lead them?”

Felough met his eyes. “No. But I'd rather you remembered why you do.”

Before more words could be said, a horn blew from the northern watch.

Not the templars.

Not the cult.

Something else.

A single figure approached the ridge, walking openly beneath a storm-black sky. Cloaked in crimson, armored in thorns of iron and bone, he carried no sigil… but his aura screamed divinity.

“Eliegh,” Valerius called calmly. “Tell me that's not what I think it is.”

The stone-clad warrior stepped forward, eyes narrowing. “It's him.”

“Who?”

Eliegh's voice was quiet, grim.

“Gavinus.”

Below the ridge...

The God of Light, son of Valdre, walked among mortals with no fear. Lightning danced along his pauldrons. His sword was not drawn. It didn't need to be. Power pulsed from him like heat from a forge.

He called up to them.

“Valerius.”

The ridge fell into silence.

“You were once a soldier. You bled in the name of order. You believed.”

Valerius stepped forward. “And I still do.”

Gavinus tilted his head. “Then kneel.”

“No.”

A pause.

“Then die.”

He raised a hand.

And the sky turned white.

Lightning tore down upon the ridge like a hammer from heaven--cracking stone, splitting earth, scattering the front line. Tiavoss raised his shield and held firm, though his armor steamed with heat. Felough rolled, pulling mortals to safety.

Eliegh fell to one knee, hand in the soil.

Valerius, sword drawn, stared through the blinding glow.

“I won't kneel,” he whispered, “but I'll let you fall.”

He charged.

The two met at the slope of the ridge, blade to blade--mortal grit against divine fury. Gavinus struck like a star burning out; Valerius moved like a man with nothing left to lose.

Around them, the army of mortals began to fight back--not with belief, but with fire, steel, and memory.

They were not chosen.

They were choosing themselves.

And at the heart of the battle, locked with a god, Valerius bled.

But so did Gavinus.

## Chapter 9: When Gods Begin to Bleed

Smoke curled over the Ridge of Darnan. Not the kind born of burning wood, but of unmaking--the unraveling of something divine.

Gavinus lay half-buried in broken earth, golden ichor bleeding from a split in his side. His sword, once radiant with holy fire, flickered weakly beside him like a dying star. The air itself trembled near his body.

Valerius stood over him, chest heaving, blade cracked and smoking.

“You should've killed me,” Gavinus rasped, voice shaking with disbelief.

Valerius shook his head. “I don't kill men who still bleed.”

Gavinus coughed, laughing bitterly. “Then you're not ready for what's coming.”

“I don't need to be ready,” Valerius said, eyes cold. “I need to be willing.”

Behind him, the mortal camp gathered slowly. Wounded. Burned. Breathing. They had seen a god fall--not dead, but humbled. And that image would outlive any victory song.

Felough approached cautiously. “What now?”

Valerius looked out across the valley, toward the distant peaks. “Now the gods know we're not asking anymore.”

Far above, in Aetherspire…

The halls of the divine shuddered.

A god had bled. Openly. Before mortals.

And still, Valdre remained seated.

“He should have burned them all,” muttered Ostheus, pacing near the flame-forged columns. “Gavinus was too soft. He let sentiment blind him.”

“He let blood spill,” Drexl corrected. “Which means they can bleed again.”

“And you're eager to prove that, aren't you?” came a voice like frost breaking glass.

Baldri, the night god, stepped from shadow, eyes dim, voice quiet.

“Leave this to the mortals,” Baldri continued. “They are only claiming what we abandoned.”

“You forget your place,” Drexl snapped.

“I never had one,” Baldri replied, already turning to mist.

Ostheus turned to Drexl. “This is a fracture. We need strength. Unity.”

“No,” Drexl said, almost whispering. “We need elimination.”

Deep beneath the sanctum of Serosh…

In the temple with no door, no priests, and no name, Serosh stood before a pool of still, black water. Visions swirled across its surface -- lightning splitting stone, a god falling, a man rising.

He was smiling.

“They think this war will be won with blades,” he said to the darkness. “But war is never about blood. It's about belief.”

The water pulsed.

Behind him, a figure emerged from the shadows -- silent, cloaked in green and black, one hand still shackled, the other trailing flame.

Breylar.

Serosh turned to face him. “It is time.”

Breylar's voice was quiet. Hollow. “You wish me to kill him.”

“No,” Serosh replied. “I wish you to tempt him.”

Elsewhere…

In the oldest forest, where time was a dream and roots whispered secrets, Ordovis opened his eyes.

The trees had told him.

The gods were falling.

And the world was beginning to choose sides.

With a sigh like wind through ancient branches, he stood, his sword creaking with moss and memory. He whispered not to a god, but to the earth itself.

“It begins.”

## Chapter 10: Ashes Between the Light

Night fell cold and quiet over the Ridge of Darnan.

The battlefield was still. No cheers. No songs. Just the faint clatter of armor being removed, wounds being bound, and the distant cry of crows that had never dared come so close to gods before.

Gavinus had been taken.

Not killed, not crowned, just… carried away by the wind, vanishing in a streak of gold and storm. The gods would lick their wounds. But the mortals--they would remember.

Valerius sat alone near a broken statue of Valdre, his cracked blade across his knees. The silence was a weight.

Tiavoss approached, his broad frame more tired than ever before. “You should rest.”

Valerius shook his head. “I don't know what that is anymore.”

Tiavoss sat beside him with a grunt. “We've done something irreversible.”

“Good.”

“No,” Tiavoss said, voice low. “Good would be if we hadn't had to do it.”

Valerius looked at him.

Tiavoss met his gaze. “You're still a man. Don't forget that when the fire starts to feel warm.”

At the edge of camp…

Felough paced. He hadn't slept. His sword still hummed faintly at his side. Eliegh leaned against a moss-covered boulder nearby, arms crossed, saying nothing.

“You felt it, didn't you?” Felough asked. “That moment… when Gavinus flinched?”

Eliegh nodded slowly.

“He was scared.”

“Not of dying,” Eliegh murmured. “Of being wrong.”

That silence lingered between them until a new presence stirred the air.

Footsteps.

Slow. Heavy. Measured.

A figure approached--alone. Cloaked in red and black, his armor burned at the edges, his face hidden behind a horned helm. He walked like a knight, stood like a god… and spoke like neither.

“I seek the one called Valerius.”

Felough drew his sword without hesitation. “You're either lost or suicidal.”

The figure stopped. “I am Breylar.”

Eliegh's eyes narrowed. “That name…”

Breylar removed his helm.

He was young. Human. Or something close to it. His eyes glowed faintly red, and veins of emberlight crawled beneath his skin.

“I was made,” he said, “by Manus.”

That sent a ripple through the air.

“I do not come to fight,” Breylar continued. “Not yet. I come with a message.”

Felough raised his sword. “Go ahead. Speak it from the end of this.”

But Breylar looked past him, locking eyes with Valerius now walking toward them, blade in hand.

“My creator does not fear the gods. He does not fear your fire, your sword, or your cause. But he watches with great interest.”

Valerius stopped, saying nothing.

Breylar's expression didn't change. “You've become something dangerous. Not because of what you've done. But because of what you might still refuse to do.”

Valerius's voice was steady. “And what's that?”

Breylar stepped forward.

“Replace them.”

The air went still.

“You fight for freedom,” Breylar said. “But what happens after they fall? Who decides the law? Who holds the sword? You, Valerius? Or someone worse?”

Valerius didn't blink. “We'll decide when we get there.”

“You may not have that luxury,” Breylar said. “The world doesn't wait for crowns to grow comfortable. It takes. It burns. It consumes.”

He stepped back and put on his helm again.

“When next we meet, it will be as reflections.”

And he vanished in fire, leaving behind only scorched grass… and a question.

Later that night, Valerius stood alone, staring at the stars.

Eliegh joined him. “What do you think he was?”

Valerius didn't look away. “A warning.”

“Do we fear it?”

Valerius's eyes narrowed.

“No.”

He looked down at his blade, cracked but still intact.

“We answer it.”

## Chapter 11: The Breaking Circle

The gods had not gathered again in full.

Aetherspire--once the heart of divine order--sat half-empty, its throne room echoing with footsteps that no longer marched in unity. The great hall was dimmer now, as if the world itself had begun turning its gaze away from those who claimed to shape it.

Valdre's throne remained unoccupied. The elder god had not spoken since Gavinus's fall.

Now only Drexl, Radeem, Ostheus, and Serosh remained. Others had retreated to their own dominions, unwilling to answer questions they had no answers for.

Drexl stood at the center, his crimson armor pulsing faintly with inner fire. “He should never have gone alone.”

“He volunteered,” Ostheus snapped, sparks flicking from his pauldrons. “And he paid for his arrogance.”

Radeem scoffed, leaning on his blade. “This is what happens when we treat rebellion with diplomacy. I'd have burned Valerius down to his bones.”

“You would've only made him a martyr,” Serosh muttered from the shadows.

Drexl turned to him. “And what would you have done, traitor? Whispered in his ear like you do in every warlord's dreams?”

“I would have let him rise higher,” Serosh replied, eyes glowing faintly beneath his hood. “High enough for the fall to be divine.”

Silence followed.

“You speak like you want them to win,” Ostheus hissed.

“I speak like someone who understands cycles,” Serosh replied. “You want to preserve a system that is already dead. Mortals don't need gods anymore. They need a reason not to become us.”

Drexl stepped forward, his hand resting on the hilt of his dark-forged blade. “Say what you mean.”

Serosh smiled. “I already have.”

Suddenly, the divine flame at the center of the spire flared violently, and a voice echoed through the air--not from the gods, but from beyond them.

“The false child has moved.”

All eyes turned toward the center as a crack appeared in the air itself--space unraveling like fabric torn at the edge.

And from the tear, stepped The Dark One.

Not fully seen. Not fully present. His form was cloaked in something older than shadow, his eyes hollow and deep as the void. His voice came as echo, as dream.

“Breylar was never yours to command. He walks a path of fire... and he will not be stopped.”

The gods did not speak.

They listened.

“You sought dominion. Now face consequence. The age of voices is ending. And in the silence, only those who burn brightest shall remain.”

The Dark One vanished, the air stitching itself closed with a sound like thunder collapsing.

Radeem was the first to speak.

“…We're losing control.”

“No,” Drexl whispered. “We're being tested.”

That night, in the wilds of Elarion…

A storm broke over the horizon.

Valerius stood watching it, his armor still marked by Gavinus's fury, the edges of his blade reforged in silence.

He felt it.

Not just the storm. But eyes.

Gods were no longer only watching.

They were afraid.

And afraid gods are more dangerous than dying ones.

## Chapter 12: The Hollow Throne

The cavern was alive with whispers--voices of the damned, of gods long dead, and of things that had never been given names.

At the center, atop a throne carved from obsidian and bone, sat Manus.

His body was a map of ruin--veins like molten cracks, eyes that no longer blinked but burned. Where he sat, the stone pulsed. Where he breathed, the air warped.

He was not dying.

He was becoming.

Beneath him, chained to the foundation of the temple, knelt a priest--once devout to Valdre, now broken.

Manus spoke, voice like earth breaking open:

“Tell me. Do you still pray?”

The priest whimpered, blood dripping from his mouth. “Y-yes…”

“To whom?”

“…no one answers.”

Manus stood. His form cracked with every movement, as if the universe itself strained to contain him.

“Exactly.”

He raised his hand. The priest vanished into ash.

The silence that followed was not empty. It was expectant.

From the far side of the cavern, Breylar approached, his armor still scorched, his expression unreadable.

“You spoke to him,” Manus said without turning. “Valerius.”

“I did.”

“And?”

“He's not ready.”

Manus nodded. “He won't have a choice soon.”

He turned to face his creation.

“I've seen the shape of the world that comes next. Not ruled by gods. Not ruled by kings. Just… ruled.”

Breylar tilted his head. “You don't want power. You want certainty.”

“I want a world where gods no longer get to choose who lives and who burns.”

“You've become one.”

Manus smiled.

“No. I've become the answer to them.”

The cavern rumbled. In the deepest hollow of the temple, something stirred--a titan's breath, long and low.

Manus looked toward the darkness.

“Soon,” he whispered.

Meanwhile, in the mortal world…

Valerius and his company reached the ruins of Sanctum's Fall--once a temple of light, now a shattered relic overtaken by moss, ash, and silence. It had once been a place of worship to Valdre. Now, it was a shell.

Felough walked beside Valerius, running his hand across the broken stonework.

“How many of these are we going to pass before we admit it?”

“Admit what?”

“That we're not just killing gods. We're erasing everything they built.”

Valerius looked up at the temple's collapsed dome.

“Then let's build something better.”

Eliegh emerged from the shadows, a scroll in hand. “I found something.”

“What is it?” Valerius asked.

Eliegh unrolled the parchment. An old map, ancient and mostly lost to time. At its center, a strange symbol--neither divine nor mortal.

Felough leaned in. “I've never seen that sigil before.”

“It's not from Elarion,” Eliegh said.

Valerius's brow furrowed. “Then where is it from?”

A voice answered from the trees.

“From beyond.”

They turned.

Andromedous stood there, his presence warping the light around him.

“You seek to unmake the gods,” he said calmly. “But something far older… seeks to unmake you.”

He stepped forward, the sigil on the map glowing faintly in his presence.

“Manus has awakened something even he does not understand.”

“And what is it?” Valerius asked.

Andromedous met his gaze.

“The first god.”

## Chapter 13: The First Flame

The night after Andromedous's warning fell like a curtain of stone. No stars. No wind. Only the heavy breath of a world remembering its own buried trauma.

Valerius stood over the glowing map, his companions gathered silently behind him. Andromedous knelt near the ancient symbol--etched not in ink, but in something older. A material that pulsed faintly, like cooled lava reawakened.

“You said this comes from before the gods,” Eliegh said.

Andromedous nodded. “Before even memory. Before Valdre raised his hand. Before Manus raised his sword. There was something else.”

Valerius's voice was quiet. “What was it?”

Andromedous looked up.

“A name spoken only in death. A presence buried in stone and silence. The First God.”

Felough stepped back, frowning. “I thought Valdre was the first.”

“No,” Andromedous said. “Valdre was the first who chose to be remembered.”

He waved a hand across the map, and the glowing sigil rose into the air, floating like a flame made of thought.

“This god was never meant to be worshiped. It was not kind. It did not guide. It simply… was. A force. A hunger. Fire without light. Void without peace.”

Valerius frowned. “And Manus has found it.”

“No,” Andromedous corrected. “It found Manus.”

Long ago…

In the time before time, Elarion was chaos. No sky. No sea. No form. Only burning dark and swirling ash.

And at its heart, something watched.

It had no face. No body. It lived in moments of destruction, in cracks between creation. It whispered into stars and waited for form to betray function.

When the gods rose, they did so not from nothing--but from its bones.

They stole fire. They carved names. They rewrote what had never been meant to be understood. And in doing so… they buried it.

They named it only once: Enoch.

Then they sealed it in the Hollow Below.

But now, the seal had fractured.

And Enoch remembered its name.

Now…

Valerius stared into the floating sigil.

“And what does it want?”

Andromedous's expression didn't change.

“It doesn't want anything. It is want itself. It is the instinct to consume. To burn. To end. It is what gods fear most--not death, not rebellion… but something they cannot control.”

Eliegh lowered his head. “And Manus has touched it.”

Andromedous nodded. “And soon, it will no longer need him.”

Valerius looked to the others.

“We thought we were fighting gods.”

Felough finished the thought.

“We're fighting what made them possible.”

Beneath the world…

Manus stood before a sealed gate--massive and ancient, covered in forgotten script. His blade pulsed. His eyes bled faint light.

He raised his hand.

The gate did not resist.

It welcomed him.

And as the stone split open, a single word filled the air. Not spoken. Not screamed. Breathed.

“Enoch.”

And in that moment, the flame that made the gods blinked… awake.

## Chapter 14: Beneath Their Thrones

Lightning carved the sky above Aetherspire, but the light did not comfort. It only revealed how much the gods had missed.

Drexl stood at the edge of the sanctum, peering down into the scrying pool--a mirror of the world below. It rippled not with battle, but with decay.

The roots of Elarion were unraveling.

And for the first time in an age, Drexl felt something he hadn't since his own rise.

Fear.

Ostheus entered in a flash of silver static, his armor scorched from distant storms. “More shrines have gone dark. The faithful no longer speak our names. Even the flame-temples of Deneath are quiet.”

Radeem appeared next, his curved sword leaving scorch marks as he leaned it against the marble. “The mortals are not the threat. This is… something else.”

“Then name it,” Drexl growled.

But none of them did.

Not until the floor itself groaned.

The throne of Valdre--untouched, unmoved since the dawn of the divine--shattered.

Cracked straight through its golden base.

Silence fell.

Then Serosh stepped out of the dark, his voice sharp as falling glass.

“It's awake.”

Drexl turned slowly. “What do you mean?”

Serosh smiled bitterly. “The god before the gods. The thing we buried. The one we refused to name. It's not asleep anymore.”

Radeem stepped back. “That's impossible. Valdre sealed it. He--”

“He lied,” Serosh said flatly. “We all did.”

Ostheus clenched his fists. “If Manus touches it--”

“He already has,” Serosh said. “And now he's not just corrupting... he's opening.”

They looked to the broken throne.

And Drexl, blood god, breaker of tyrants, whispered, “Then we need to kill Manus now.”

“No,” Serosh said. “We need to kill Enoch.”

“But how do you kill something older than death?” Radeem asked.

Serosh looked away.

“You don't.”

Meanwhile…

In the ruins of Sanctum's Fall, Valerius sat beside a fire with the ancient scroll still clutched in his hands. Andromedous had retreated into silence. The map glowed faintly in the dirt beside him. Across the way, Tiavoss tended to the wounded, and Felough sharpened his blade with haunted focus.

Eliegh knelt beside him. “You saw it, didn't you?”

Valerius nodded. “It was like… looking through the world. Seeing what the gods tried to hide.”

“We weren't supposed to find this,” Eliegh said. “We weren't supposed to know.”

Valerius looked toward the dark horizon. “Then it's exactly what we need to understand.”

The fire cracked, casting long shadows across the temple stones.

“Tomorrow,” Valerius said, rising to his feet, “we march north.”

“To where?” Felough asked.

“To the Hollow Below,” Valerius said. “If Enoch is real--if it's rising--we don't wait for it to come to us.”

He looked at each of them.

“We find it. We face it. We end it.”

Even if it kills us all.
