# Neo Ciara

*The story of two nobodies who sold a gun to the wrong man, the city that decided to make an
example of them, and the very old, very strange world that had been quietly living
underneath Neo Ciara's neon the entire time.*

---

## Prologue: A Shining and Dark Metropolis

By 2082, Neo Ciara had stopped being a city and become an argument — one side lit up in
Nexus-blue and gold, arcology towers stacked like something out of a fever, the other side
sunk in the runoff shadow underneath, where the light never quite made it down past the
fortieth floor.

It was a city for all sorts and manners of people, the way any city that grows fast enough
becomes one — corporate executives who never touched the ground, augment junkies who'd
traded away pieces of themselves for pieces of an edge, street preachers, street dealers,
gang colors that changed meaning block to block, and an old underground that most of the
new arrivals never learned existed at all.

Because that was the part nobody advertised on the Nexus billboards: Neo Ciara held more
than dark secrets. It held wisdom considerably older than the city itself, older than the
chrome and the neon and the corporate seal stamped over half the skyline — wisdom that had
been quietly living in the city's oldest bones since long before anyone thought to build a
city there at all, waiting, the way old things wait, for someone to need it badly enough to
go looking.

Two nobodies were about to need it very badly indeed.

---

## Chapter One: A-D and Fitch

Arthur Diego hadn't gone by that name in fifteen years, not since he was young enough to
run away from a home he never talked about and never looked back toward. Neo Ciara raised
him instead — badly, unevenly, the way a city raises anyone who arrives with nothing — and
what came out the other side called itself A-D and answered to little else.

He'd grown up on the street level with a kid named Fitch, and the two of them had stuck
together with the specific, unbreakable loyalty of people who'd had nobody else stick to
them first. A-D was the muscle when muscle was needed — not because he loved using it, but
because Fitch, all wit and no weight, couldn't handle anyone with a bigger frame than his
own, and somebody had to. Fitch was the mouth, the plan, the hustle so smooth he could rob a
man blind and have him smiling on the way out the door. Neither of them would have said the
word out loud, but they were, as much as either of them had, family.

By 2082 they'd settled into a living neither of them would have called glamorous: small-time
arms dealing, moving product between people who couldn't ask too many questions about where
it came from, ripping off a fair number of their clients along the way and dealing with the
consequences whenever those consequences caught up.

It wasn't much. A-D was perfectly happy to live it anyway.

That was, right up until the day they sold to the wrong guy at exactly the wrong time.

---

## Chapter Two: The Wrong Buyer

The deal itself was nothing special — a case of augment-grade sidearms, cash up front, the
kind of transaction A-D and Fitch had run a hundred times without either of them thinking
twice about who was buying or what they planned to do with it.

What made this one different was where the guns ended up: in the hands of a Ciara Locos
crew, on a corner that belonged, by every informal treaty the gangs still bothered to
honor, to the Crux.

The gunfight that followed wasn't supposed to happen. Gunfights on contested corners rarely
were. But somebody's nerves were bad, or somebody's orders were worse, and within an hour
three blocks of Neo Ciara's lower ring were lit up in exchange fire loud enough to bring
Nexus Corp's own security response instead of the city's overworked and underinterested
police.

That was the part nobody in the underworld had accounted for. Gang violence was background
noise to Nexus, tolerated the way weather was tolerated. Gang violence loud enough to
threaten a Nexus-owned distribution corridor was an entirely different category of problem,
and problems in that category got Cletus's personal attention.

A-D and Fitch heard about the fallout the way most of the city did — secondhand, through
rumor, already three steps behind the people deciding what was going to happen to them.
Neither of them yet understood that they were about to become the answer to a question
Nexus hadn't finished asking.

---

## Chapter Three: The Twins Who Run the City

Cletus understood, better than almost anyone left in Neo Ciara, that his actual job had
stopped being CEO of Nexus Corporation a long time ago. His actual job was running the city
itself — the last real bastion, by his own quiet reckoning, of a kind of civilization that
the rest of the world had let slip through its fingers, and he had no intention of letting
Neo Ciara slip with it.

His twin brother Cahill ran the other half of that operation, and ran it, if anything, more
effectively. Where Cletus commanded through ownership — towers, contracts, the quiet
leverage of controlling half the city's power grid — Cahill commanded through faith. He
stood behind the pulpit of the city's largest cathedral, in a district that had somehow kept
its old stonework standing among all the new steel, and explained the twins' more
questionable decisions as divine and blessed, decisions handed down from somewhere technology
couldn't reach.

People bought it. Hook, line, and sinker, most of the time — and Cahill had a theory about
why, one he never fully said aloud even to Cletus: that when technology takes over every
other facet of a person's existence, the only thing left worth believing in is something
that can't possibly be quantified. Faith didn't compete with Nexus's power. It filled the
one gap Nexus's power couldn't reach.

Between the two of them, the brothers controlled a considerable share of the city's actual
influence, and had taken it upon themselves — for better and, more often than either would
admit, for worse — to make sure Neo Ciara stayed exactly the way they liked it. When a few
bad eggs started messing with that arrangement, the twins made sure there was hell to pay.

Cahill did wish, sometimes, that his brother would show up for the church more. He was
still more than happy to facilitate whatever plans Cletus had in mind, though, telling
himself — the way people who help run cities often tell themselves — that it was all for
the betterment of the place they'd bothered to grow up in.

---

## Chapter Four: Gary's Bargain

Gary had led the Ciara Locos for six volatile years, and if his appearance — mohawk dyed
whatever color caught his eye that week, augment scars worn like trophies instead of hidden
like most people hid them — wasn't already a dead giveaway, anyone who spent ten minutes
with him walked away certain of one thing: Gary was, by a comfortable margin, the craziest
man they'd ever meet.

He hadn't always run a street gang. A long time before the colors and the war with the
Crux, Gary had run with a private security outfit, disciplined work under a commander he'd
once have died for. That life ended the day Gary made a choice he never fully explained to
anyone in the Locos, a betrayal that sent both himself and his former commander down a path
neither of them ever found their way back from. Gary buried the details of it the way he
buried most things that still hurt: under noise, under chaos, under a gang war against the
Crux that gave him somewhere to point all that unresolved violence.

That war was the thing Gary actually cared about by 2082 — right up until the gunfight on
the contested corner brought Nexus down on his neck personally.

Cletus didn't threaten. Cletus didn't need to. He simply made clear, in the specific and
bloodless language of a man who owned half the city's infrastructure, exactly how thoroughly
he could unmake the Ciara Locos if their leader didn't offer something useful in exchange
for being allowed to keep existing.

Gary offered A-D and Fitch. The two would-be gun-runners who'd supplied both sides of the
fight, an easy, deniable answer to a question Nexus badly wanted closed.

He told himself it was practical. He didn't let himself examine, too closely, how much it
resembled the last time he'd sold out someone who trusted him to survive something.

---

## Chapter Five: Reeva's Hunt

Reeva was Gary's right hand and the Locos's collector, an agent of chaos with twin
bone-blades mounted along her forearms and a smile that made people confess to things they
hadn't done yet just to make it stop. Her assignment was simple: deliver A-D and Fitch to
Nexus, alive enough to be useful, and neither of them would see it coming.

They didn't, at first. The chase started as an ordinary flight through the lower ring's
maze of market stalls and stacked containers, Reeva always one turn behind and closing,
until it stopped being ordinary somewhere in the flooded transit tunnels beneath Third
Sector, where running out of room to run meant finally having to fight instead.

It wasn't skill that saved them, not entirely. It was Fitch's wit catching, at the last
possible second, on the one weakness Reeva's own crew joked about behind her back — she
never fought well near live current — and A-D's willingness to put his own body between a
blade and his friend one more time, the way he always had, without needing to be asked. Bad
luck, good instincts, and a severed power conduit bought them exactly long enough to
incapacitate her and disappear into the tunnels before Nexus's actual response team arrived.

Reeva woke up in Nexus custody instead of Locos custody, which she understood, immediately
and correctly, as a death sentence. She had failed the one job that mattered. Cletus didn't
deal personally with failures that small.

He sent Floret instead.

---

## Chapter Six: What Made Floret

Floret didn't enjoy killing Reeva. That was, in its own bleak way, the most human thing left
in him — that after everything Nexus had done to his body over the years, he still felt the
weight of what he did, even when he did it without hesitation.

He'd been private security once too, decades back, running the same kind of crew Gary had
run, before Gary had run it into the ground. Floret had watched, over those years, the worst
humanity had to offer come through in a slow, grinding parade, and had watched his own body
fail to keep pace with a young and wild crowd he no longer fully understood. He'd let Nexus
augment him, piece by piece, trading flesh for reliability, until even he could no longer
say with certainty where the man ended and the machine began.

It was Gary's betrayal that finished the job. His own lieutenant, someone Floret had trusted
with his life more times than either of them had bothered to count, had sold him out to save
himself — the specific old wound Gary still refused to name to anyone in the Locos. Floret
survived it. What survived wasn't entirely Floret anymore. What Nexus rebuilt out of the
wreckage became their go-to enforcer: teched up, unforgiving, the fixer they called whenever
they or the church had a problem that needed to disappear.

He killed Reeva efficiently and without ceremony, a debt collected on Gary's behalf whether
Gary understood it that way or not, and turned his full attention to the two would-be
gun-runners who were, by every account reaching Nexus Tower, proving considerably more
resourceful than their file suggested.

Nothing was going to stand in his way. He'd made very sure of that, one augment at a time,
for years.

---

## Chapter Seven: The King Out of Time

A-D and Fitch made it three more days on nothing but momentum and Fitch's talent for finding
doors nobody else remembered existed, before momentum ran out in a dead-end alley off the
old transit spine, Floret's silhouette closing from one end and nowhere at all to run at the
other.

That was when the man in the hood found them first.

He moved like nobody they'd ever seen move — not fast, exactly, but certain, the specific
economy of someone who has fought so many kinds of impossible thing that an augmented Nexus
enforcer barely registered as a new category. He didn't introduce himself before putting
himself between the two of them and Floret. He didn't need to. He simply held the line long
enough for Floret to reconsider the odds and withdraw to report back, and only then turned
around and said, in an accent that belonged to no part of Neo Ciara anyone had ever heard,
that his name was Kleon, and that they were coming with him.

His base of operations was dingy, off the radar, and — as they discovered over the following
days — considerably better stocked than its exterior suggested. Kleon called himself the
city's vigilante, its self-appointed protector, though he never fully explained, at first,
why a man who moved and spoke the way he did had chosen Neo Ciara specifically, out of every
century and city available to him.

He introduced them, in time, to the people who kept his corner of the underworld
functioning: Reese, a healer and spiritual guide with open contempt for the church and a
gentler touch than anyone expected from a man who dressed like he'd wandered out of a
different century entirely, and the Serpent-King, self-proclaimed ruler of the underground
scene, who dealt less in violence than in the much rarer currency of safe passage for anyone
who needed to disappear.

"You're not the first strays Nexus has tried to grind down to nothing," Kleon told them, the
first night, watching Reese work on the cut across Fitch's ribs. "You won't be the last.
Doesn't mean you have to be the next."

Neither A-D nor Fitch understood, yet, exactly what kind of world they'd just been pulled
into. They would, before this was over, understand it far too well.

---

## Chapter Eight: The Assassin's Contract

Floret's retreat wasn't Nexus giving up. It was Nexus escalating, the way Nexus always
escalated: quietly, efficiently, and through someone considerably harder to see coming.

RT-599 didn't have a name that predated the designation, or none that Nexus's records
bothered to preserve. What Nexus's payroll bought was simpler than a name: a blade discipline
that had survived, unbroken, out of some older tradition nobody in the corporate towers
understood or particularly cared to, wrapped around a mind that had been trained, methodically,
to feel nothing about the work at all.

The contract was straightforward. Kill the two gun-runners. Make it quiet enough that the
story never had to compete with Nexus's own version of events.

RT-599 came close, twice, in the weeks that followed — once in the market stalls, where only
Serpent-King's warning network gave A-D and Fitch the two minutes' head start that saved
them, and once in the tunnels themselves, where Kleon's own blade, older and stranger than
anything RT-599's discipline had trained against, turned an ambush into a stalemate neither
side walked away from satisfied.

"Nexus doesn't stop," Kleon told them afterward, cleaning a wound that would have killed
anyone whose body worked the way an ordinary body was supposed to. "Not for gunrunners. Not
for gang wars. Nexus stops when something costs more to keep pursuing than it's worth to
have caught."

None of them yet knew how completely that math was about to change — not because of
anything Nexus decided, but because of what Cletus was about to become desperate enough to
do.

---

## Chapter Nine: The Desperate CEO

The gang war Cletus had assumed he could contain didn't stay contained. Gary's grip on the
Locos frayed under the pressure of a leader everyone could see was fighting a war against
Volkov's Crux and losing ground on two fronts at once — the Crux themselves, and his own
inability to deliver the two gun-runners Nexus had been promised weeks ago.

Volkov, for his part, watched Nexus's attention swing toward his rivals with the patient,
calculating satisfaction of a man who had spent years building the Crux into something more
disciplined than Gary's chaos, and began, quietly, positioning to take advantage of it.

Public unrest crept in behind both of them — the kind of unrest that made even a city as
thoroughly owned as Neo Ciara nervous, small fires that threatened, if left too long, to
become one large one. Cahill's sermons started running longer, more elaborate, working
harder to explain away a situation that was getting more difficult, week by week, to explain.

Cletus felt the city's grip on itself loosening for the first time since he'd taken Nexus's
chair, and did what desperate men with resources have always done: he went looking for
something more powerful than anything Nexus's conventional arsenal could offer.

What he found, buried in records the corporation had quietly acquired and never fully
understood, was a name out of a much older war, sealed away and, by every account, safely
dead: Malakar.

Cletus told himself, making the arrangements, that he could control what he was about to
wake up. Every desperate man who has ever reached for the darkest tool available has told
himself exactly that.

Malakar was reborn in the sub-levels beneath Nexus Tower, and the first thing the lich
understood, opening eyes that had not opened in longer than Neo Ciara's oldest buildings
had stood, was that the man who had called him back had absolutely no idea what he'd
actually done.

---

## Chapter Ten: The New Vorath

Malakar did not announce himself to the city. That had never been his way. He simply began,
patiently and precisely, to look for the thing that would hurt the people who'd woken him
the most — not out of any particular grudge against Cletus specifically, but out of the old,
practiced instinct of something that had learned, across a very long unlife, that the fastest
way to break a person is through whoever they love.

Cletus didn't love anyone in a way Malakar could easily use. Kleon, whose meddling Malakar
sensed the moment he surfaced — an old, familiar signature from a war Malakar half-remembered
fighting through — was harder to reach directly. But Kleon had gathered people around him,
and one of those people had exactly the kind of bond Malakar knew how to turn into a weapon.

Fitch was taken from the underworld's tunnels on a night A-D wasn't there to stand between
him and whatever came for him — the first time, in fifteen years of friendship, that Fitch
had needed exactly the thing A-D always provided and hadn't had it.

What came back, days later, wasn't Fitch. Malakar's corruption didn't work the way ordinary
violence worked. It didn't simply hurt him. It reached into everything Fitch had ever been
— the hustle, the wit, the loyalty, the contempt for anyone who'd ever looked down on him —
and twisted all of it into something with teeth, a title that had claimed other victims in
other ages and would claim this one exactly the same way: Vorath, the newest bearer of an
old and hungry curse, killing machine, wearing the last true friend A-D had in the world like
a coat he no longer needed to take off.

A-D found him in the ruins of the market district, three days after the taking, and spent
one full minute believing he was looking at Fitch before the thing wearing Fitch's face
opened its mouth and made a sound that had nothing left of a friend inside it.

He didn't kill it that night. He couldn't. He ran, the way Kleon had taught him to run when
running was the only option left, and carried out of that market district a grief that
would not, for a very long time afterward, find anywhere safe to put itself down.

---

## Chapter Eleven: Two Years of Death's Hand

Vorath did not stay Vorath for long. Whatever Malakar had built inside Fitch kept evolving,
the way the curse always evolved when it was given enough time and enough violence to feed
on, until the thing hunting Neo Ciara's underworld had a new name and considerably less left
of the man it had started as: Death's Hand, and for two years, it did not stop.

Two years is a long time to be hunted by something that used to know exactly how you thought.
Death's Hand anticipated Kleon's routes before Kleon had finished planning them. It found
Serpent-King's safe passages half a step behind every time the underground rerouted them. It
took allies A-D had barely had time to learn the names of, and left just enough of each one
behind to make sure the message landed: this would not stop, and there was no version of
running that outlasted it forever.

Reese kept what remained of the resistance alive through those two years the way a healer
keeps anything alive under siege — not by winning, but by making sure there was always
enough left standing to try again tomorrow. A-D changed in ways he didn't fully register
happening, the wide-eyed loyalty that had defined him since childhood grinding down, day by
grinding day, into something harder and far more tired.

It was in the third month of the second year that Kleon noticed DR3 behaving strangely.

The old protocol bot had watched Kleon's perimeter faithfully for longer than anyone still
living could account for — a relic Kleon had kept running out of habit more than necessity,
never quite explaining, even to himself, why he'd never decommissioned something so
thoroughly obsolete. Something in its old, buried code had started responding to Malakar's
presence in the city the way a struck bell responds to another bell tuned to the same note,
faint at first, and then less faint with every month Death's Hand kept hunting.

Kleon began to suspect, watching it, that DR3 was not simply old. He began to suspect it
was waiting.

---

## Chapter Twelve: What the Old Ones Answer

Two years of losing ground taught Kleon something he hadn't fully accepted in all his time
in Neo Ciara: that this fight had stopped being one a vigilante, two exhausted gun-runners,
and a healer with a grudge against the church could win on their own.

He went looking for older help, the way a king who has outlived his own century learns to
go looking — through channels that had nothing to do with the city's networks, and
everything to do with debts and reputations that predated Neo Ciara by longer than anyone
alive could properly measure.

Magni and Modi answered first. Sons of a thunder god who had wandered through this world
once, long before either of them were born, and left behind, along with the stories, two
sons who took war and worthiness exactly as seriously as their father had. Magni came
eagerly, treating the fight against Death's Hand as precisely the kind of worthy contest his
blood was built for. Modi came more reluctantly, less interested in glory than in what he
called, more than once, a good end — the kind of ending a two-year siege badly needed and
had not yet found.

Andromedus was harder to reach. He had grown weary of humanity decades before Kleon ever
came looking, weary in the specific, bone-deep way of something that has spent millennia
testing mortals and watching most of them fail the test, and had retired from all of it, fully
intending never to be called upon again. Kleon called upon him anyway. Andromedus came, not
because the summons moved him, but because he recognized, in the shape of what Malakar had
become, an old and unfinished account he had never quite closed.

It was DR3 who surprised all of them.

The old protocol bot had been drawing closer to something for months, its buried code
stirring every time Death's Hand drew near, and in the same week the rest of the old allies
gathered, it finally broke open entirely — chassis cracking along seams nobody had known
were there, old plating falling away to reveal something considerably more than a sentry
bot had ever been built to hold. What stood up afterward called itself DR3-XL, and claimed,
with a calm that unsettled even Kleon, to be a reincarnation of a name several of the
gathered allies recognized immediately and none of them had expected to hear again: Drexl
Voss.

He did not explain, at first, how a legend out of Valkorath's oldest wars had ended up
folded into a sentry robot on the far side of an ocean and several thousand years. He simply
said that Malakar had crossed his path before, more than once, in more than one body, and
that he had been waiting, in whatever quiet way a piece of old code could wait, for exactly
this chance to finish something between them properly.

---

## Chapter Thirteen: Waking a God

Even with Magni, Modi, Andromedus, and DR3-XL standing alongside what remained of Kleon's
resistance, the math of the fight still didn't favor them. Malakar, comfortable and growing
in his stolen century, had spent two years consolidating power through Nexus's own
infrastructure — using Cletus's ambition, and later Cletus's terror, as a conduit into
systems no ordinary army could touch.

Reese was the one who suggested the last resort, quietly, the way he suggested most things
that made other people uncomfortable: that Neo Ciara's own oldest hidden power, the tech god
who'd first been born in an entirely different century's industrial revolution and had gone
quiet sometime before any of them arrived in this fight, might be worth waking.

Paradigm had died once, in circumstances none of the gathered allies fully understood,
somewhere in the long gap between an older war and this one. Bringing a god back from that
kind of ending required more than any single person in the room could offer. It required all
of them — Kleon's old authority, Andromedus's ancient weight, DR3-XL's borrowed legend, Magni
and Modi's inherited divinity — poured together into a working none of them were fully
confident would hold.

It held. Paradigm returned to a world he barely recognized, older technology than his own
grown up strange and sprawling all around him, and understood, within minutes of waking, why
he'd been called back and what it was going to cost to finish the job properly.

Corthos came back the same way, later that same night, though what emerged from the ritual
was less a resurrection than a correction — the old Titan's essence, harvested and twisted
by Malakar in a war centuries removed from this one, finally pulled free of that corruption
and restored to something closer to what he'd been before it happened. Corthos did not
thank them extensively for it. He simply picked up the fight the way he'd apparently been
waiting, in whatever form his stolen essence had survived in, to finally rejoin.

They had, by the end of that night, exactly what Kleon had been assembling for two years
without fully admitting that was what he was doing: enough.

---

## Chapter Fourteen: Malakar's Court

Malakar had made Nexus Tower's lower levels his own by the time the assembled allies came
for him — a throne room built from stolen infrastructure and older, darker magic layered
over Cletus's original ambition until the CEO himself had become little more than a
mouthpiece the lich no longer bothered using.

The battle that followed was not clean, and it was not quick. Death's Hand met them first,
faster and crueler than it had been at any point across its two-year hunt, and it was A-D —
not Kleon, not any of the old and powerful allies gathered around him — who finally stood in
front of what had once been Fitch and understood that the only mercy left to give was the
one he'd been avoiding for two years.

He did it himself. Nobody else was going to carry that weight for him, and he wouldn't have
let them if they'd tried. Whatever last flicker of Fitch remained inside Death's Hand seemed,
in the half-second before the end, almost grateful.

Malakar met the rest of them with everything two years of consolidated power had bought him,
including Patchwork -- the corporation's own absorption program, built around a dead
researcher's psyche and thrown into the fight to soak up whatever abilities it could reach.
It ran out of anything worth absorbing partway through Magni and Modi's opening assault,
unmade with the same detached equanimity its namesake would have found interesting rather
than tragic. It was DR3-XL who finally closed the account Drexl Voss had apparently been keeping open
across more centuries than anyone else in the room could properly follow — an old, personal
grudge settled in a fight that mattered considerably more than either of them let on.
Paradigm and Corthos broke the lich's hold on Nexus's systems from the inside, unmaking the
throne room's stolen infrastructure piece by piece.

Andromedus was the one who finally landed the blow that ended it — the ancient tester, weary
of humanity, called back one final time to render one final judgment. It cost him everything
he had left. He died in the wreckage of Malakar's court having spent, on one last mortal war
that was never truly his to fight, the very last of a life that had already outlasted
several worlds.

Malakar fell. Whether he stayed fallen, the way something that old and that patient ever
truly stays anything, was a question none of them were foolish enough to answer with
certainty.

---

## Epilogue: The King's Retirement

Corthos, restored fully to himself in the wreckage of the fight he'd helped end, had one
last thing to offer before the dust of Nexus Tower's lower levels finished settling.

He found Kleon standing over what was left of the battle and told him, in the quiet,
deliberate voice of something that had spent centuries relearning how to speak without
gears grinding underneath the words, that he had a gift to give — the only kind of gift a
being who understood time the way Corthos understood it could offer a king who had spent
lifetimes stranded outside his own.

A one-way trip through the stream Kleon had vanished into so long ago, chasing Gothar, never
returning. A chance to finally retire in the time he was meant to.

Kleon did not accept it immediately. He looked, for a long moment, at what remained of the
people he'd gathered around him in this borrowed century — A-D, hollowed out by two years of
grief and one final mercy; Reese, already moving through the wreckage looking for anyone
still breathing; Magni and Modi, already arguing amiably about whether the fight had been a
worthy one. He had built something here, in a place that was never supposed to be his.

But a king out of time is still, underneath everything, a king who has a home he never
stopped trying to get back to. He said his goodbyes the way a man says goodbye to something
he genuinely loved and still has to leave, and stepped through the doorway Corthos opened
for him, back toward a Valkorath that had been waiting considerably longer than two years
for its philosopher-king to finally come home.

Neo Ciara went on without him, the way cities always go on — Nexus Tower slowly rebuilt
under new management neither Cletus nor Cahill fully controlled anymore, the Locos and the
Crux finding, in the aftermath of something considerably larger than either gang, an uneasy
reason to stop killing each other over corners that suddenly mattered a great deal less.

A-D stayed. He didn't have anywhere else that felt more like his than the streets that had
raised him, even now, even hollowed out the way he was. He kept the arms trade going,
smaller and more careful than before, and never quite stopped listening, on quiet nights,
for a voice that used to fill every silence in the room before it had reason to.

The city held onto its dark secrets and its older wisdom both, the way it always had,
waiting — the way old things wait — for whoever needed it next.
