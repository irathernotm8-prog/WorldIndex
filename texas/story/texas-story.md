# Texas

*A grand tournament in the heart of the Lone Star State, and the story of a man who didn't know what he was until he had no choice but to find out.*

---

## Prologue: The Road Home

Montana had been gone from Texas for six years when the flyer found him.

He didn't seek things out — things found him, which was the natural consequence of being the kind of person who walked into a situation the moment he noticed it was wrong and walked out the other side without ever quite settling anywhere. He'd been in three countries in the last year alone, doing the specific kind of travelling that isn't really travelling so much as following injustice from one address to the next. He didn't call it that. He just said he went where the road went.

The flyer was taped to a telephone pole outside a diner in New Mexico, advertising the Grand Lone Star Tournament — open bracket, international field, prize pool in the hundreds of thousands, and a talisman of unspecified origin and unspecified value that the organisers were calling the real prize. Montana read it twice, not because it was unclear but because he was deciding something.

He had extraordinary strength and always had — he knew that much about himself, had known it since he was a kid growing up nomadic across the Southwest, had learned very early to be careful with it and not ask questions about where it came from. He had never entered a tournament in his life. He did not like spotlights, trophies, or any context in which the word *represent* appeared in the same sentence as his name.

He ate his breakfast and then drove east toward Texas, which was as close as he ever came to making a considered decision.

---

## Chapter One: Old Business

Brock was already in Fort Worth when Montana arrived, which was either coincidence or the universe having a sense of humour. Probably the second thing.

They had grown up together in the informal way that nomadic kids grow up together — intersecting lives, shared camps, the specific bond that forms between two people who are each other's most reliable competition. Brock had always been the strongest person in any room Montana wasn't in, and he had never entirely made peace with the amendment required by Montana's presence. A reluctant admiration was the most accurate description of his face when Montana walked into the hotel lobby with dust on his boots and a grin that said he had absolutely no idea Brock would be here.

"You're entering," Brock said. Not a question.

"Apparently," Montana said.

They shook hands the way two people shake hands when what they actually want is to immediately establish who has a better grip. Neither of them gave the other the satisfaction.

Kia was at the registration desk. She had been one of Montana's oldest friends for long enough that seeing her here wasn't the surprise — the expression on her face was. Kia was measured, precise, always three steps ahead of whatever room she was in, the kind of fighter who made you feel like the outcome had been decided before you threw your first punch. She looked like she was here for something specific and not inclined to discuss it over dinner.

Montana asked anyway.

"There's someone entering this tournament who killed my father," she said. "I intend to find out which one it is."

Montana did not ask if she was sure. He had known Kia long enough to understand that she did not bring things to him unless she was sure. He asked if she wanted company and she said she could handle it, and he said he knew that, and she said then why are you offering, and he said because you'd do it for me, and she looked at him for a moment and then went back to the registration form.

He took that as a yes.

---

## Chapter Two: The Tournament Begins

The venue was an arena twenty miles outside Fort Worth, purpose-built and huge and loud in the way that Texas things tend to be loud — not obnoxiously, just completely, as if the concept of half-measures had simply never been proposed and rejected so much as never been considered.

Two hundred fighters from thirty countries. Single-elimination bracket over five days. The prize pool was real and substantial. The talisman sat in a glass case in the lobby and looked, to Montana's untrained eye, like an old disc of carved stone about the size of a dinner plate, which was not what he'd expected from an item being described as worth more than the cash.

Drexl noticed it first.

He and his apprentice Elmarie appeared in the lobby on the morning of the first bracket day, moving through the crowd with the specific unhurried quality of people who are not in a hurry because they have already assessed everything and found it manageable. Drexl was a monk of the old kind — not quiet in the way that suggested emptiness, but quiet in the way that suggested something very full held very still. Elmarie followed him with the energy of someone who found her master's calmness mildly infuriating and had learned to work with it anyway.

Montana ran into them at the bracket board. Drexl looked at the talisman case, looked at Montana, and said something that seemed to be directed at Elmarie about patience. Then he shook Montana's hand and told him to be careful about who he let close in the later rounds.

Montana asked what that meant.

"It means what it means," Drexl said, pleasantly, and went to find his first opponent.

It meant, as Montana would eventually understand, that the tournament had been designed around the talisman, not the other way around, and the people who designed it were already inside the bracket.

---

## Chapter Three: Shin and Dylan

The first person to give Montana genuine difficulty was Shin, which caught him off guard because Shin looked like the kind of fighter you appreciated aesthetically while being fairly confident you could handle him.

You could not, in fact, simply handle Shin.

His style was built on provocation — not trash talk, but something more physical and more deliberate, little tactical irritations designed to pull an opponent's anger to the surface where it could be exploited. He used his hands in ways that weren't quite strikes, touches and redirects that were just infuriating enough to break a fighter's rhythm without technically doing anything wrong. Against someone with Montana's strength and no particular patience for being annoyed, it was an excellent approach.

It didn't work.

Montana was brash, not stupid, and the distinction mattered. He took three rounds of provocation, felt himself starting to tip, and then did what Kia had told him to do years ago in circumstances she couldn't have anticipated: he stopped trying to beat his opponent and started trying to understand him. By round four he had Shin's pattern down and was working with it rather than against it, which Shin found, gradually and then all at once, completely disorienting.

They shook hands at the end with genuine mutual respect, which is the best outcome a fight can produce when both people are actually good at what they do.

Dylan appeared by Montana's shoulder about thirty seconds after the brackets cleared.

He was from Boston and he had the specific quality of someone who had decided that America was worth representing and had committed to it in every aspect of his self-presentation. He won his fights in the first two days with the kind of uncomplicated patriotic intensity that Montana found simultaneously admirable and slightly exhausting. He liked fighting. He liked America. He liked Montana with the immediate conviction of someone who had decided in the first five minutes of a drinking contest that this was a person worth knowing.

"You fight like you're angry about something," Dylan told him, which was more observant than Montana had expected.

"I fight like I'm trying not to break anything," Montana said.

Dylan thought about this. "Same thing, kind of," he said.

Montana allowed that he had a point.

---

## Chapter Four: Stugard and the Field

The German was efficient, serious, and deeply unimpressed by the ambient noise of the entire proceedings.

Stugard fought in round two with the precision of a man who had spent a long time proving that composure was a technical advantage, which it was. He beat his opponents cleanly, expressed no opinion about anything that wasn't directly relevant to the next match, and made very clear — to Brock, who had the misfortune of being seated next to him during a break — that he found the general level of American brashness suboptimal.

Brock had thoughts about this that he kept to himself with visible effort.

Stugard was eliminated in round three by a fighter who had studied his pattern more carefully than Stugard had studied his opponent's tempo, and he accepted the loss with the same composure he'd brought to everything else. He left the tournament that day. He did not stick around to see how it ended.

What remained in the bracket after three days was the interesting part: Montana, Brock, Kia, Dylan, Drexl, Elmarie, Shin — who'd been eliminated but was now watching from the stands with the expression of someone taking notes — Lorenzo, Louis, and Sir Henry, who had not appeared on the bracket at the start and whose arrival on day three produced a noise from the crowd that suggested they had been expected by someone, just not announced.

And the Shin-Ju.

---

## Chapter Five: The Shin-Ju

Montana had been watching the Shin-Ju fighters for two days before he understood what he was watching.

Fehz was the first to make it obvious. He fought with purple Ki — dark, coiling, deeply unpleasant to be near — and he fought the way cruel people fight when they've been given permission: with extra time on each exchange, drawing things out past the point where the outcome was in doubt because the outcome wasn't the point. The crowd didn't love it. The tournament organisers apparently hadn't specified that you had to make it quick.

Drexl watched Fehz fight and his face went still in a way that Elmarie recognised and Montana did not. The monk said nothing until the match was over, and then said, quietly, that the Shin-Ju weren't here for the prize pool.

Zhang Chen, the clan's financial face, operated with the syphon ability — draining his opponent's strength across the length of a fight until what had been a contest became something else entirely. He wore a suit to matches and removed the jacket before stepping onto the mat and put it back on immediately after, which was either a deliberate statement about the kind of man he was or simply what happened when you had been a businessman long enough that the suit became structural.

Zhu did not speak. He had not spoken to anyone in the tournament, as far as Montana could tell. He fought without his sword — it hung on his back and stayed there — and absorbed damage that should have ended fights with the patience of something that had stopped tracking its own limits a long time ago. It was said that he had been an honorable man once. Looking at him, you could almost see it — something in the way he held himself, even now, that had not entirely been overwritten by whatever the Shin-Ju had done to him.

Montana watched all of this and put it together slowly and then said to Kia: the talisman. They want the talisman.

"The talisman enhances abilities," Kia said. "A Shin-Ju member with the talisman —"

"Don't say it like that," Montana said. "Because then we have to do something about it."

Kia looked at him.

"We're going to do something about it," he said.

---

## Chapter Six: Lorenzo and Louis

Montana discovered Ki watching Lorenzo fight.

The Italian moved like a dancer and fought like a storm, green energy coating him in a way that looked decorative until you understood what it was doing — amplifying every strike, absorbing impact, giving him a kind of fluid invincibility that made his actual fighting style, which was already excellent, simply overwhelming. He fought his brother Louis in the quarterfinals and it was the most technically beautiful and emotionally complicated thing Montana had seen in the tournament.

Louis was blue Ki, fencing-precise, carrying the specific grievance of someone who needed to prove something and had never quite been given the opportunity. They had the same inherited ability from an English father they shared and had never agreed about anything else, and watching them fight was watching two people speak the same language about completely different things.

Montana watched Lorenzo work and understood something.

He had always been stronger than he should be. Not trained stronger — constitutionally stronger, the way some people are just faster without being taught speed. He had never looked too closely at where it came from because the looking had always felt like the beginning of a question he wasn't ready to have answered. But watching Lorenzo coat himself in green energy and watching that energy come from *inside* rather than outside — from the same place that Montana's strength had always come from — he felt something shift.

He didn't say anything about it to anyone. He sat with it for a night and tried to understand what he was sitting with.

Sir Henry intervened the next morning, which Montana hadn't asked for and probably needed.

---

## Chapter Seven: Sir Henry

The father of Lorenzo and Louis made his entrance on the fourth day like a man who had been waiting to make it for a long time and had spent that time deciding exactly how.

He stepped onto the mat in a suit that had been expensive in 1995 and was merely distinguished now, and he summoned red Ki in a way that made both of his sons go absolutely still in their seats, because they had never seen their father fight and had never known, until this moment, that he could. The ability came from him — the whole bloodline came from him — and he had never told either of his sons this, which was the kind of omission that looked different depending on which son you were.

He fought with the specific grace of someone who had been the best at something for a long time and had not needed to prove it until now, and the red Ki moved around him like something alive and attentive. He won his first match. He won his second.

He sought out both sons that evening. Lorenzo first, then Louis, in private conversations that Montana heard about secondhand and in pieces that didn't quite add up to the whole. He was trying to reconcile them. He was trying to explain something that had needed explaining for years and had not been explained because pride and distance and the specific stubbornness of men who believe that eventually the right moment will appear.

The right moment doesn't appear. You have to make it.

He made it, imperfectly, and it was not enough — the conversations ended the way conversations between people with old wounds often end, with acknowledgement rather than resolution — and Sir Henry stepped back onto the mat the next day knowing it hadn't been enough and intending to keep trying.

Fehz killed him.

It was outside the tournament, after hours, which was how the Shin-Ju operated when they decided that a fighter was a specific problem rather than a general obstacle. Sir Henry had apparently said something to Drexl about the talisman's origin that Drexl had then said something about to the wrong person, and Fehz received an instruction and carried it out.

Montana found out the way you find out about most things that have already happened — too late, from Elmarie, whose face was doing something careful to hold very still.

Lorenzo and Louis were told separately, and came together in the hallway outside the tournament medical room, and stood there without speaking for a long enough time that the thing that had been keeping them apart had nothing left to stand on.

It was, Montana thought, watching from the end of the hallway and not intruding, exactly what Sir Henry had been trying to achieve. Just not the way he'd intended.

---

## Chapter Eight: Drexl

Drexl's death was different.

He had known it was coming in the way that very calm, very perceptive people sometimes know things — not prophecy, not premonition, but the plain consequence of having assessed the situation completely and found that one outcome was considerably more likely than the others. He had entered the tournament with Elmarie to stop the Shin-Ju's plan, not to win, and stopping the plan required staying in the bracket long enough to get close enough to understand exactly what the talisman was and why the clan wanted it.

He'd gotten close enough. Zhang Chen had identified him as the specific threat he was.

Zhu was sent.

The fight happened in a corridor between matches, which was not how tournaments were supposed to work and was very much how the Shin-Ju worked. Montana arrived in time to see the end of it — in time to see Drexl, who had fought brilliantly and lost to the straightforward problem of someone who simply would not stop regardless of what you did to them, sitting against the wall with the calm acceptance of a man who had calculated this as one of the acceptable outcomes.

Elmarie was already there.

"You knew," she said, not as an accusation. As a question that had already found its answer.

"I knew it might go this way," Drexl said. He looked at her with the specific attention of someone who has not wasted attention throughout his life and is not wasting it now. "That's why I spent ten years teaching you everything I know and not holding anything back."

He did not say anything else. He didn't need to.

Montana stood in the corridor for a while after Elmarie had said what she needed to say to him, and then he went to find the rest of what he needed to do.

---

## Chapter Nine: Ki

Kia found him in the practice room at eleven at night, which was where he'd been for three hours.

He was not practising anything recognisable. He was standing in the middle of the room with his eyes closed and his hands at his sides, doing something internal that she couldn't read from the outside.

"Montana."

"I'm trying to find it," he said.

"Find what."

"The thing Lorenzo has. The thing Sir Henry had." He opened his eyes. "I've always had something. I just never... looked at it directly."

Kia was quiet for a moment. She was not the kind of person who offered reassurance when the more useful thing was silence.

"Drexl knew," Montana said. "He said be careful about who you let close in the later rounds. He wasn't talking about tactics."

"He was talking about what you'd discover if you made it to the later rounds," Kia said.

"Yeah." He closed his eyes again. "I'm trying to find it."

It came, slowly, the way things come when you stop trying to force them and start trying to invite them. A warmth that had always been there, that he'd always expressed as strength without understanding what it was. It did not have a colour yet. He didn't know what colour it would be.

He found out two days later, in the fight against Zhu, when he didn't have any choice left.

It was white.

Not bright white — not the white of blank things — but the white of something very old and very settled, the way a stone that's been in a riverbed for centuries is white. It came off him without drama, without the green flourish of Lorenzo or the blue precision of Louis or the red elegance of Sir Henry. It came off him like something that had been waiting a long time to be acknowledged and was not making a big deal of the acknowledgement.

Zhu stopped when he saw it. Montana would remember that — the undead warrior, bound to a clan that dictated his every move, stopping for just a moment and looking at something that had found its way out into the open air after a long time underground. Some honour remained in him. The stopping was the last of it.

Montana beat Zhu with the ki and his own hands, which was the only way Zhu was ever going to be beaten.

---

## Chapter Ten: Heksh

The final wasn't in the bracket.

Heksh stepped onto the mat before the scheduled final could begin and dismissed the remaining structure of the tournament with the complete indifference of someone who had been using it as a delivery mechanism from the start. He had the talisman — Zhang Chen had retrieved it the previous night, and the Shin-Ju operatives who'd been embedded in the tournament staff had cleared the way. He held it now and it was doing something to him that was visible from twenty feet away: not changing him so much as amplifying what was already there, which was already enormous.

The grand monk of the Shin-Ju wanted complete dominion of the fighting world. This was stated simply, without flourish, as the opening of the only speech he gave. Then he stopped talking because talking had always been optional to him.

What came next was not a tournament match.

Montana went first and lasted longer than expected because the white ki was doing something useful against whatever the talisman was amplifying. He was not winning. He was buying time.

Brock came in without asking. This was the most Brock thing that had ever happened — no announcement, no coordination, just the simple arithmetic of a man built for exactly this scale of problem deciding that the time for waiting was over. He hit Heksh with everything a human body could generate and it was, genuinely, considerable. It was not enough.

Kia came in next. She was three steps ahead, as always, and she had been watching Heksh move for five minutes before she engaged, which meant she had things to work with that the others didn't. She was not winning either. She was finding the edges.

Dylan was bleeding and came in anyway because he had decided that America was worth representing and that this was, in fact, the situation in which that decision meant something.

Elmarie came in because Drexl had spent ten years giving her everything he knew and she was going to spend it on the thing he'd come here to stop.

Lorenzo came in with green Ki blazing, and Louis came in on the other side with blue, and they moved in the same rhythm for the first time in their lives without discussing it, which was probably not what Sir Henry had imagined when he pictured them reconciling but was very much in the spirit of it.

Kai came back.

Elmarie's younger brother, who had run off after she beat him, who had gone to the Shin-Ju because Drexl had chosen her and then had found, at the edge of the final fight, that the Shin-Ju had killed the man who'd made that choice. He came back with his flaming fists and said nothing to anyone and went directly for Heksh's right side, which was the specific contribution that the specific shape of his ability made possible.

Montana found his opening.

It was small — the kind of opening that exists for one second between the work of everyone around you and then closes again — and he went through it with everything he had, which now included the white ki that had been waiting in him for thirty years to be used for something that deserved it.

Heksh went down.

The talisman skittered across the mat. Nobody picked it up for a long moment. Then Kia walked over and picked it up, which was practical and correct.

"What do we do with it," Brock said.

"Not give it to anyone who wants it," Montana said.

There was general agreement on this.

## Epilogue: Texas

The arena was quieter in the hour after it was over.

Most people had cleared out in the chaos following the final. What remained was a specific group of people with various levels of injury and various relationships to each other, sitting in and around the ring in the way that people sit after something large has passed through them.

Lorenzo and Louis were talking. Actually talking — not circling, not performing, just talking. Montana watched this without intruding and thought about Sir Henry, who had wanted exactly this and had not gotten to see it. Some things you arrange and don't get to attend. That was the particular sadness of it, and it was also, he thought, probably how Sir Henry would have wanted it to be arranged — through necessity rather than sentiment, because his sons were both too proud for sentiment to work on them and had needed something larger.

Kai sat apart, which was appropriate. He had earned his place in the fight and would need to earn his place in the aftermath separately, at his own pace. Elmarie would handle that. She handled most things.

Dylan was talking to Brock about something that seemed to involve both of them being patriots in adjacent but different ways, and Brock was listening with the specific expression of a man who has decided that the argument isn't worth having today and will be there tomorrow.

Montana picked up his hat from the mat where it had ended up during the fight and put it back on.

"You could have won the bracket," Kia said. "Clean. Before the Shin-Ju moved."

"Probably," Montana said.

"So now what?"

He thought about the white ki that was still there, settled again now, the riverbed stone back under water. He thought about being thirty-six years old and finding out that the thing he'd always thought of as an attribute was actually something more specific than that, something with a tradition and a bloodline and presumably a history he didn't know.

"Now I go find out what it is," he said.

"And then?"

He looked at the arena, at Texas through the open doors, at the road that went wherever it went.

"Then I probably end up in a different kind of trouble," he said. "You know how it goes."

Kia allowed that she did.
