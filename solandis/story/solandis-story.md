# Solandis

*The story of the last Templars of Solandis, the war they thought they understood, and the
shadow that was never Tiadel at all.*

---

## Prologue: The Cross Before the Cross

Long before armored men in a colder, younger world would paint a red cross on white and call
it holy, the shape belonged to Solandis.

It was a small kingdom, wedged between the mountains and the sea, in the years before anyone
called this age Valkorath — though the word already existed, already meant something, already
named the crown to the north that Solandis paid tribute to and prayed would remember it
existed. Solandis had never needed the Valkorath Crown's help before. It had its own order of
knights for that: men and women bound less by blood than by a vow, who wore white and bled
red and called themselves, simply, the Templars.

Nobody remembers who painted the first cross. The story the Templars told each other — in
chapter houses, around campfires, to frightened recruits who wanted to believe their armor
meant something — was that the cross was older than the order itself, older than Solandis,
a mark some long-dead founder had taken from a dream and never explained. It didn't matter
if it was true. It became true the moment enough Templars died wearing it.

To the north, Tiadel had hated Solandis for longer than either kingdom kept honest records
of. Old wars, older grudges, a border that had moved so many times it had worn a scar into
the land itself. Everyone assumed the current war was simply the latest chapter in that
long, tired argument.

Everyone was wrong about that. It would take the Templars years to find out how wrong, and
by then it would be far too late to matter.

## Chapter One: The Grandmaster's Order

Sigurd had led the Templars of Solandis for eleven years, and in that time he had learned
that being right and being loved were rarely the same job.

He was not a warm man. He did not pretend to be. He made decisions the older knights
grumbled about and the younger ones didn't understand, and he let them grumble, because a
Grandmaster who needed to be liked was a Grandmaster who would eventually be talked out of
something necessary. What Sigurd had — what even his critics admitted he had — was the
absolute, unshakeable belief that the order would survive him. Not because he was
irreplaceable. Because he'd built it to not need him.

"You could ask them instead of telling them," Baldric said, watching a dozen new recruits
drill in the yard below Sigurd's tower window. Baldric was the closest thing Sigurd had to
an equal in the order — a knight entrenched in Solandis so completely that people sometimes
forgot he'd had a life before the white and red, before the vow, before any of it.

"I ask them things I don't already know the answer to," Sigurd said. "This isn't one of
those."

Among the recruits was a boy named Non, seventeen and earnest in the specific way of
someone who has not yet been given a reason not to be. He'd grown up in the shadow of the
chapter house, had wanted to be a Templar since before he understood what the word cost,
and drilled harder than anyone else on the field because he still believed effort was the
thing that decided outcomes.

"That one," Baldric said, nodding at him. "Reminds me of someone."

"Reminds you of everyone at seventeen," Sigurd said. "Ask me again in ten years whether he
still looks like that."

Baldric didn't answer. He already had someone at home who looked at him the way Non looked
at the drill-master — his own apprentice, younger even than Non, who had attached himself to
Baldric two winters ago with the specific devotion of a boy who has decided, against all
evidence that devotion is dangerous, to have a hero anyway.

## Chapter Two: Flame and Kindness

The order's newest division trained apart from the others, in a yard scorched black from
years of careful, deliberate fire.

Pyre had been the first Templar to bend flame to the order's purposes, years before anyone
called it a division or gave it a name. He'd started as a swordsman like any other. Then
Tiadel's raids had grown stranger — fewer soldiers, more fire, villages burned in patterns
that didn't look like accidents — and Sigurd had made a decision that split the chapter
house down the middle: if Tiadel had started using something like sorcery, Solandis would
need an answer that wasn't only steel.

Pyre had volunteered before anyone finished asking.

He'd been given his own division and his own scorched yard and, slowly, his own reputation:
the Templar who carried fire the way other men carried grudges, close and careful and never
quite at rest. He didn't love what he'd become. He loved that it was useful.

"You'll set the whole yard alight one of these days," Galateya told him, arriving with two
water skins and the particular grin she reserved for people she was fond of, which was
nearly everyone.

Galateya was, by wide agreement, the finest blade in the order — and, by even wider
agreement, one of the kindest people in it, a combination the older knights liked to say
shouldn't be possible and clearly was. She fought the way Sigurd led: without apparent
effort, without apparent doubt. She was, more than anyone else still standing, what the
Templars had told themselves they were for — before the wars got long enough to make people
forget.

"The yard's already black," Pyre said. "Nothing left in it to alight."

"That's what you said last season."

"I was wrong last season too."

She sat with him while he worked, the way she did most evenings, because Pyre burned things
carefully but not people gently, and someone needed to remind him, periodically, that he
was still one of the latter.

## Chapter Three: What Came Out of Tiadel

The raid on Fenmarch should have been ordinary. It wasn't.

Sigurd rode out with two dozen knights, Baldric and Galateya among the first riders,
Tryfus — a broad, laughing swordsman with a reputation for ending fights before they
properly started — leading the second column. Non rode with the reserves, too green for the
front line and furious about it in the quiet way that recruits learn not to say aloud.

Tiadel's raiders were where the scouts said they'd be. That much was ordinary. What wasn't
ordinary was how they fought — with a coordination Tiadel's usual border raiders had never
shown, and, in the final minutes of the engagement, a silence. Men who should have been
screaming, wounded and dying, made no sound at all. They simply stopped, the way a puppet
stops when something lets go of the strings.

Tryfus was the one who noticed it first, because Tryfus was closest when the silence
started. He didn't say anything about it that night. He would, eventually. Just not soon
enough, and not in a way anyone wanted to hear.

Sigurd stood over the strange, silent dead for a long time after the fighting ended.

"Tiadel raiders don't fight like that," Baldric said.

"No," Sigurd agreed. "They don't."

Neither of them said the other thing out loud — that whatever this was, it hadn't come from
Tiadel's side of the border so much as through it, the way water comes through a crack
without caring whose wall it was.

## Chapter Four: The Hollow Court

Sigurd sent word north within the week. It was not the first time.

The Valkorath Crown had a name for what Solandis kept asking for — aid, attention, the
acknowledgment that a tributary kingdom's war was also, eventually, the Crown's problem —
and the name was *pending*. King Valerius III had been called many things by many people
over his long, ceremonial reign, but efficient had never been among them. He was not cruel.
He was simply somewhere else, always, even when he was in the room: a king more occupied
with how history would remember him than with the small, unglamorous work of being
remembered well.

Solandis's letters went into the same slow current every kingdom's complaints went into,
and came back, eventually, as silence.

"He'll answer when Solandis is a story instead of a kingdom," Sir Roger said, not
unkindly, because Sir Roger rarely said anything unkindly — it was simply the shape the
truth happened to take, this time.

Sir Roger was old enough to have trained half the senior knights still standing, Sigurd
included, and had long since stopped expecting the world to reward good behavior. He'd
outlived two Grandmasters before Sigurd and fully expected the pattern to continue. It
didn't make him bitter. It made him useful in exactly the way an old soldier who has run
out of illusions is useful: nothing left to protect but the thing actually worth protecting.

Alaric, who still believed the Crown would come through if only someone explained the
stakes clearly enough, found this endlessly frustrating.

"Someone should tell him what's actually happening down here," the boy said, meaning
Valerius, meaning the strange silent dead at Fenmarch, meaning all of it.

"Someone has," Baldric said. "Several someones. He's not confused, lad. He's just decided
this isn't his war yet."

"Then whose is it?"

Nobody answered him. Nobody had an answer yet worth giving.

## Chapter Five: Tryfus

The second strange raid took Tryfus's sword arm, nearly took his life, and returned him to
the chapter house a full week later than the scouts who'd gone looking for him expected.

He said he'd crawled the whole way. He said a lot of things, in the weeks after, that
didn't quite fit together — where he'd been, how the wound had closed so fast, why he
flinched now at ordinary firelight the way he'd never flinched at Pyre's division training
a hundred feet from his own quarters.

Galateya was the one who sat with him most, because Galateya sat with everyone, and she was
the first to say, quietly, to Sigurd, that something in Tryfus's eyes had stopped looking
entirely like Tryfus.

Sigurd didn't act on it fast enough. He would carry that, after.

It happened in the refectory, at midday, in front of forty knights who would spend the rest
of their lives disagreeing about exactly what they'd seen. Tryfus stood up from his bench
mid-sentence, and something that was wearing his laugh but had stopped being his laugh some
time before that moment put a blade through the knight beside him before anyone had
finished registering that he'd moved.

It took six Templars to bring him down, and it was Galateya, in the end, who did it —
who put her sword through the friend she'd nursed back from the second raid, weeping while
she did it, because kindness in Solandis had never meant softness, only that the cost of a
thing was always paid in full and never pretended away.

"Another force," Sigurd said, standing over what was left. He didn't say Tiadel. For the
first time since Fenmarch, out loud, in front of witnesses, he didn't say Tiadel at all.

## Chapter Six: The Shape in the Smoke

Non saw him first, though he wouldn't understand what he'd seen for years, if ever.

It was during the third raid — the one on the eastern watchtowers, the one that finally
convinced Sigurd the letters north were a waste of good parchment. Non had been posted to
the ridge above the tower line, close enough to see the fighting, too far to reach it in
time to matter, which was its own kind of lesson about the war he was in.

Through the smoke, behind Tiadel's lines, where no commander of any ordinary army had
business standing, there was a figure in black that the fire didn't seem to touch. Non
watched it for the length of one indrawn breath — tall, still, watching the battle the way
a man watches a pot he is confident will boil on its own schedule — and then the smoke
closed over it and it was gone.

He told Baldric. Baldric told Sigurd. Sigurd, to his lasting credit, believed him
immediately, because by then belief was cheaper than the alternative.

"Tiadel doesn't have commanders who stand still," Baldric said.

"Tiadel doesn't have commanders the fire won't touch," Sigurd said. "Whatever that was,
it isn't wearing Tiadel's colors because it belongs to Tiadel. It's wearing them because
they're convenient."

Nobody in that room ever learned the shape in the smoke had a name. Nobody learned that the
war eating Solandis alive had started, years before Fenmarch, as one quiet, patient
experiment by a man the wider world would come to call Lord Drexl — a man testing, in the
grinding attrition of a border neither kingdom controlled, exactly how much of an army he
could turn without anyone noticing whose hand was really on the reins. Tiadel took the
blame because Tiadel was standing where the blame was easiest to place, and a war fought in
someone else's name is, for the man actually fighting it, the safest kind there is.

Solandis knew only that something was using their oldest enemy as a glove. They never
learned whose hand was inside it. That knowledge would cost more than any of them had left
to spend.

## Chapter Seven: What the Walls Cost

The end, when it came, did not come slowly.

It came in a single season, the way a wound that has been quietly infected for months will
suddenly, all at once, take the whole limb. Tiadel's forces — or the thing wearing Tiadel's
forces — moved on the capital with numbers Solandis's scouts hadn't accounted for and
couldn't explain, and Sigurd, for the first time in eleven years as Grandmaster, gave an
order that was not a question and not up for grumbling: everyone who could not fight was to
be gone from the city by nightfall.

Pyre and his division held the eastern gate long enough for the evacuation to clear the
lower quarter, and then long enough after that for it to clear the second quarter too, and
by the time anyone thought to tell him he'd done enough, there was no one left to hear it.
The scorched yard he'd trained in for years was nothing, in the end, compared to what he
left of himself at that gate. It bought hours. Hours were the only currency left that
mattered.

Galateya died on the west wall, where she had gone — over a direct order from Sigurd, which
she ignored for the first and only time in her service — because the retreat route ran
past a nursery she had visited every week for six years, and she was not going to let it
empty into the street unescorted. She got every child out. She did not get herself out. The
knights who found her after said she was still smiling, in the specific, exhausted way of
someone who has finished the only job that mattered to her and is content with the trade.

Sigurd held the north gate himself, because a Grandmaster who would not stand where he sent
others was not a Grandmaster worth the vow, and because eleven years of being right instead
of loved had never once meant he was wrong about what the job actually required of him. He
did not survive the holding. The order he had spent a decade making sure would not need
him turned out, in the end, to need exactly that: someone willing to prove it, at the gate,
with his life.

## Chapter Eight: Sir Roger's Fire

Sir Roger found the shape in the smoke outside the shattered south wall, in the last
hour before dawn, and understood — the way old soldiers sometimes simply understand things
without being told — that this was the actual war, and everything before it had only been
the length Solandis had been made to run before reaching it.

He did not ask its name. It offered one anyway, almost as a courtesy, the way a man might
introduce himself to someone he intends to kill and finds he respects regardless.

"Drexl," it said, and for the first time, Sir Roger understood the shape of every strange
silence, every raid that fought like nothing Tiadel had ever fielded, every ounce of the
war that had never made a shred of honest sense until this exact moment.

He did not have Sir Tyrus's youth, or Sir Tyrus's future, or two companions to lend their
strength to his. What Sir Roger had was sixty years of service and the specific, terrible
clarity of a man who has already decided what he is willing to spend and has simply been
waiting for something worth spending it on.

He burned his own life to meet Drexl's strength, the old Templar rites his order had
guarded and rarely used because the cost was exactly what it sounds like: not power lent,
but power taken from the only place left to take it. He fought longer than anything wearing
that much darkness should have allowed. He drew blood that would not close quickly, on a
thing that did not know, yet, how to be afraid of dying — a wound Drexl would carry, unseen,
into every fight that followed, including one, years later, against a golden knight named
Tyrus who would finish what Sir Roger had only started.

It was not enough. Sir Roger had known, riding out, that it would not be enough — that
enough had stopped being available to Solandis several chapters before this one. He died
with the cross still on his chest and nothing in his hands but the truth, which nobody left
alive would learn from him, because there was nobody left alive close enough to hear it.

He died with his honor and his ideals fully intact. It cost him everything else. In the
ledger the Templars had always kept, unspoken, that had never once been recorded as a
losing trade.

## Epilogue: What Walked Out of the Ashes

Baldric walked out of Solandis with a wound that never fully closed and a grudge that
never fully cooled, Alaric half-carrying him the last several miles, still, impossibly,
telling him it would be all right — that the Crown would answer eventually, that someone
would account for what had happened here, that belief was not yet a foolish thing to carry.

Baldric let him believe it. It was the only inheritance he had left to give the boy.

Non walked out with them, quieter than either, the effort-shaped optimism of the training
yard burned down to something narrower and harder, a young man who had learned that trying
was not, in fact, the thing that decided outcomes, and had not yet decided what to
replace that lesson with. History would lose track of him not long after. It loses track of
most survivors, eventually, once the story stops needing them.

None of the three ever learned Drexl's name. They carried Tiadel's instead, all the way to
whatever came next — Baldric into a long, wandering search for a reckoning with a king who
would never give him one directly; Alaric into a devotion that would, years later and far
from here, be turned against everything he'd once believed in, in an instant, the way
devotion can be when it has never been given a true target to aim at; both of them,
eventually, into the wider story of Valkorath, carrying a fallen kingdom's cross into a war
that was, in the end, the same war all along.

Far behind them, in the wreckage of the south gate, something wounded and patient gathered
itself back into the shape of a man, and began, unhurried, to heal.

Solandis had been the first proof of concept. It would not be the last. And somewhere to
the north, in gold armor he had not yet been forced to earn, a knight named Tyrus was
still years away from learning that his name had already been written into the ledger of
a war he did not yet know he was fighting.
