# London

*The story of Gareth, of Deek, and of the city that made them understand what they were actually made of.*

---

## Prologue: Before

Gareth had a theory about London.

The theory was that every city had a specific quality to the darkness it generated — not the absence of light, but the particular character of what moved in it — and London's had always felt older than the city itself. Like something that predated the buildings and had simply let them grow up around it, the way water lets you build a bridge without caring much about the bridge. He'd been in London twice before, briefly, selling things to people in back rooms and moving on. Both times he'd felt it. Both times he'd decided not to think about it too carefully.

He was twenty-six when Deek called. He was working a contact in Prague, trying to offload a Byzantine reliquary of uncertain provenance, and his phone rang at eleven at night and it was Deek, which meant whatever it was couldn't wait.

"I found something," Deek said.

"You're always finding something."

"Something big."

Gareth was quiet for a moment, looking out the window at the Prague skyline. He had a rule about jobs that were described as big, which was that they always were, and not always in the way the word was meant.

"London?" he said.

"London," Deek said.

He took the next flight.

---

## Chapter One: The Artifact and the Address

Deek met him at Heathrow with a folder and the expression of a man who has found something that has made him less certain rather than more.

The folder contained photographs of an artifact — a black stone seal of the kind used in medieval signet traditions, except the markings on it weren't medieval and weren't any tradition Deek could identify, which was a short list. He'd acquired it through a contact in Whitechapel who'd moved it from a private collection that had changed hands three times in two months in ways the paperwork didn't fully explain. He'd traced the acquisition chain back to an address. The address felt wrong.

"Felt wrong how," Gareth said.

"Like it knew I was looking," Deek said.

This was not the kind of thing Deek said. Deek was precise and military and assessed threats in concrete terms. Gareth took the folder and looked at the photographs and felt the specific chill of something that was genuinely old — not old like the reliquary in Prague, which was old the way furniture was old, with a history and a value and a purpose that made complete sense. Old like the dark outside the buildings, like the thing that had been here before the city.

"What's at the address," Gareth said.

"That's what we're going to find out."

---

## Chapter Two: Mariella

They found Mariella the same night they found the address, which in retrospect should have been more suspicious than it was.

She was lifting something from a building three streets over from the Whitechapel address — not the artifact, something else, something that had been poorly secured in a way that suggested the people securing it were focused on other threats. She was extremely good at what she was doing and would have been invisible if Gareth hadn't been on the wrong rooftop at the wrong time and watched the whole thing with the professional appreciation of someone who had been on the other side of that kind of work.

She noticed him noticing.

"American," she said, not as an accusation, just as a category.

"Artifact hunter," Gareth said.

She looked at the thing in her hands and then back at him with the slight assessment of someone deciding how much effort a situation required. "There's a lot of those lately," she said. "Someone's been buying heavily."

She came with them because Deek pointed out that a blood mage who knew the local black market was more useful than the alternative, and because Mariella agreed with the logic in the easy way of someone who was already moving in the same direction for her own reasons. She was warm and funny and the blood magic she used with such offhand comfort was genuinely alarming in ways that she seemed to find others' alarm about it mildly entertaining.

Gareth liked her immediately. Deek trusted her provisionally. Neither of them was wrong, then.

---

## Chapter Three: Black Jack

The first sign that they'd been noticed was Black Jack.

He appeared on a Tuesday, outside the pub they'd been using as a base, and the black ooze that moved off him in slow tendrils was the first time Gareth had seen the substance in person. He had read about things like it in the kind of sources that aren't cited in academic papers. Reading about it and watching it reach toward you from a very large man in antique clothing were usefully different experiences.

They ran.

This established the rhythm of the next two weeks: Black Jack appearing at inconvenient moments, close enough to remind them they were being tracked, not quite close enough to finish it. He had the patience of something that had been hunting things for a long time, and he used it. The ooze gave him a reach that made direct confrontation inadvisable. He hunted them across the city with the specific deliberateness of someone who had been told to send a message rather than end it.

The message was clear: they had found the edge of something, and the something had noticed.

"He's not trying to kill us," Deek said, after the third encounter. "He's trying to see where we go."

"Comforting," Gareth said.

"Somewhat," Deek said.

---

## Chapter Four: Leopold

Leopold found them, or rather allowed himself to be found, which turned out to be a distinction that mattered.

He presented himself as a researcher of the occult — the modern kind, with institutional backing and a network of contacts and a respectability that his anachronistic aesthetic slightly undermined. The armour he wore to meetings that didn't require armour was either an affectation or a statement, and he was charming enough that the question never quite surfaced directly.

He knew the Black Order. He knew the Whitechapel address, the seal, the acquisition chain that had moved the artifact through three collections. He knew the history of the ooze — where it came from, what it did to the people who carried it, why the Order used it so selectively. He was extraordinarily useful.

He helped them for three weeks. In those three weeks they got closer to understanding the Order's structure than they had in all the time before. He introduced them to contacts, opened doors, translated documents that would have taken Deek months. He was good company. He seemed to genuinely enjoy their company.

Gareth found himself trusting him in the automatic way that you trust someone who has repeatedly done the thing they said they would do.

He would think about that specific trust for a long time afterward.

---

## Chapter Five: Sergei

Sergei appeared mid-investigation like a fact that had always been true and simply hadn't been mentioned.

He was tracking the Order from a different angle — a Serbian intelligence thread that had connected the London operation to something his government needed contained for reasons he didn't explain and they didn't push. His methods were different from theirs: quieter, more systematic, less inclined toward the kind of improvisation that Gareth had always treated as a professional skill. He assessed situations in terms of acceptable outcomes and acted accordingly.

Their goals overlapped enough for a working relationship and not quite enough for trust. Mariella found him interesting in the slightly unsettling way she found most things interesting. Deek respected his methodology while finding his priorities limited. Gareth thought he was probably the most competent person in any room he entered and said so, which Sergei received without visible reaction.

They worked together for ten days. On the eleventh day the situation shifted in a direction that Sergei's mission parameters didn't account for, and he made his assessment and stepped back, cleanly and without apology. His government's interest was in intelligence, not in the resolution of a power struggle that had acquired dimensions he hadn't been sent to manage.

"You're leaving," Gareth said.

"My job is done," Sergei said.

"Ours isn't."

"No," Sergei said. He looked at Gareth for a moment with something that might, in a different person, have been regret. "Good luck."

He was gone the next morning. Gareth never quite got over it, which was less about Sergei specifically and more about what his leaving meant about the moment they were in.

---

## Chapter Six: Sir Elliot

Sir Elliot appeared at night the first time, which Gareth registered as threatening and then revised when the second and third appearances also came at night, always at night, always just on the edge of visible.

The first two times he said nothing and was gone before they could press him. The third time he spoke, briefly: that they were getting close to something, that getting close to it was dangerous, and that they should be more careful about the people they were trusting.

They took this as a general warning. They should have taken it as a specific one.

After Leopold's betrayal, Sir Elliot came to them directly for the first time and told them who had sent him. A benefactor. Someone who had been watching the Black Order for decades and had decided that having someone on the ground in London with a blade specifically made for the ooze was a useful investment. He didn't give the benefactor's name. He didn't need to — there was a pattern to this kind of long-view interference that Gareth would only fully recognise years later, sitting in a New York park watching the city wake up.

He joined them without making a thing of it. He carried a dark blade that moved through the ooze like it had been designed for exactly that purpose, which it had. He was quiet and precise and exactly as useful as a phantom-like figure with a purpose-built weapon should be.

Gareth liked him. It was that kind of story.

---

## Chapter Seven: Leopold's Turn

The betrayal was spectacularly well-prepared, which was the thing that made it land the hardest.

They were deep into the Order's network — close to Augustus, close to the source of the ooze's power in London, close to whatever the Order had been building toward — when Leopold stopped helping and started directing. The re-routing was subtle for the first few minutes and obvious by the time it was irretrievable. He'd been moving them into position across weeks of apparently genuine assistance, and the position he'd moved them into was one that served the Order rather than exposed it.

Then he showed them the ooze.

He had it. He had always had it. He had never needed to use it until now because being useful was more effective than being threatening, which was a lesson Augustus had apparently taught him well.

The fight that followed was bad. They got out, barely. They got out because Mariella's blood magic proved surprisingly effective as a disruptive force against the ooze at close range and because Sir Elliot cut through two of the Order's operatives in the time it took Gareth to understand what was happening.

They got out. They lost ground they'd taken weeks to gain.

Deek didn't say anything about it that night. Gareth didn't say anything either. Sometimes a betrayal is big enough that the only appropriate response is silence for a while.

---

## Chapter Eight: Clark

They found Clark, or rather stumbled into him, in the tunnels beneath an Order property in the East End.

Augustus had given him the deal weeks earlier — power, in exchange for service, in the language that men who want things from desperate people use when they want to dress the transaction up as opportunity. Clark had taken it because his options had been a single point and this was the only door open and you don't ask too many questions about a door when the alternative is the wall.

The ooze had consumed most of him by the time they encountered him. What remained was a creature of enormous and entirely indiscriminate power, spilling darkness through the tunnels in waves that made navigation nearly impossible. And underneath it, visible if you knew how to look — or if you were Gareth, who had spent enough time looking at things he wished he hadn't seen to have developed the skill involuntarily — Clark. Still there. Still conscious. Not wanting to be doing this.

The fight was long and expensive and at the end of it Gareth had three cracked ribs and Mariella had used more blood magic than she'd wanted to and Clark was gone.

Gareth sat against the tunnel wall for a few minutes and didn't speak.

"He wasn't in control of it," Deek said quietly.

"I know," Gareth said.

"That matters."

"I know," Gareth said again.

It mattered. It didn't help.

---

## Chapter Nine: Augustus and Maximus

Augustus was everything his reputation suggested — distinguished, courteous, genuinely brilliant, and completely without the part of a person that connects intelligence to conscience. He received them in the Order's main location, which they'd finally found and breached, with the specific composure of a man who had spent decades managing situations more dangerous than this one and found composure to be his most effective tool.

He had Maximus.

Maximus was what you got when the Order reached into an old grave and pulled out a name without pulling out the person who'd owned it. A shape, silent, moving with the economy of something that had no instinct left except purpose. He was extraordinarily powerful and entirely indifferent to self-preservation, which made him more dangerous than anything else they'd faced because the things that worked against the others — momentum, fear, the instinct to survive — didn't apply.

He killed Sir Elliot quickly. This was the sequence: Elliot moved to intercept Maximus, Maximus did not slow, and then Elliot was down and the blade was on the floor and Gareth was looking at the body of a man he'd known for two weeks who had died doing exactly what he'd come here to do.

Gareth picked up the sword.

He did not think about it. He picked it up because it was there and because the thing in front of him was still moving and because Sir Elliot had told him, on the third night they'd worked together, that the blade would work for whoever carried it. That had seemed like an odd thing to say at the time and now it didn't.

He killed Maximus with the dark blade, which took everything he had and left him standing in the Order's main hall with cracked ribs and a dead man's sword and Augustus watching from across the room with the expression of someone making a rapid reassessment.

Augustus released Vorath.

---

## Chapter Ten: Vorath and the Fall of Augustus

Vorath was the last resort and he killed Augustus first, which was the oldest kind of justice and also the least satisfying because it meant they still had to deal with Vorath.

The creature was ancient and furious and moved through the hall like something that had been waiting for centuries to do this and was not interested in taking its time now that the moment had arrived. He had no interest in the hierarchy of who was enemy and who wasn't — Augustus had released him and was therefore indistinguishable from everyone else who needed to not exist anymore.

Augustus died before he could make it to the door.

Gareth killed Vorath with Sir Elliot's sword in a fight that lasted longer than any fight had a right to last given the state he was in, and at the end of it the blade was still in his hand and the hall was quiet and Deek was saying something about getting out before whatever came next arrived.

They got out.

The Black Order, without Augustus and without Maximus and without Vorath and with most of its London operation in ruins, collapsed into itself the way organisations collapse when the architecture is gone — not in a single dramatic moment but in a series of smaller failures, quietly, over the weeks that followed.

It should have been over.

---

## Chapter Eleven: The Blood Queen

Mariella waited until the Order was beaten.

This was tactically correct, and it was the last tactically correct decision she made in London.

She revealed herself in the street outside the Order's hall, in the grey early morning with the city beginning to surface from the night around them, and what she revealed was that she had always been the Blood Queen, that the warmth and the fun and the genuine enjoyment of their company had all been real and that none of it had changed the fact that she'd been working her own agenda since before Gareth arrived. The Black Order had accumulated power across centuries. She had come to take it. They had been excellent at clearing the path.

She cut off Gareth's arm because he was in the way and because she needed thirty seconds.

She took the power.

The final confrontation happened two days later because Deek had tracked where she'd gone and because Gareth had two days to make peace with having one arm and was not particularly close to peace but had made a decision about what he was going to do with the next several hours regardless.

She killed Deek.

It was the logical move. Deek was the one who had found her, was the one tracking her, was the one whose methodical assessment of the situation was the actual threat to her position. She was correct about this. She killed him efficiently, which was the only way Deek would have accepted going — he would have been contemptuous of anything that took longer than necessary.

Gareth killed her with Sir Elliot's sword.

She was more powerful than him and had both arms and had the Black Order's accumulated power behind her and she underestimated what grief does to a person who decides that grief and purpose are the same thing. He killed her in the specific, cold, entirely committed way of someone who has moved past the question of whether they can do this and arrived at the question of what comes after.

He didn't know the answer to that question. He stood in the street with one arm and a dead man's sword and his best friend gone and waited to find out.

---

## Epilogue: After

He left London on a Thursday, which was the kind of day you left cities on — not dramatically timed, not a clean break, just the day the practical arrangements were complete enough to allow for departure.

He had one arm. He had Sir Elliot's sword, which he kept because he didn't know what else to do with it and because it had earned the right to be kept. He had a profound and newly-arrived understanding of the specific cost of trusting people in situations where trust is the most efficient way to destroy you.

Deek had a flat in Edinburgh. Gareth cleaned it out over the course of a day, carefully, the way you do something that deserves attention. He found notes. He found the folder from Heathrow with the photographs of the seal. He found a letter that Deek had started writing at some point during the investigation and not finished, addressed to someone Gareth didn't know, in the brisk and slightly formal way Deek wrote everything.

He didn't finish reading the letter.

He got a prosthetic arm eventually — and then a better one, and eventually one that worked well enough that most people didn't notice until they were close — and he went back to work because the alternative was stopping, and stopping was something he had decided not to do. He was more careful than he used to be. He trusted people less. He was louder and more bravado and more narcissistic than he had been at twenty-six, because it turned out that building a version of yourself that led with confidence and kept everything valuable well behind it was a functional way to move through a world that had demonstrated what it did with things you actually cared about.

He thought about Deek often. He thought about Mariella less, which was its own kind of loss — the version of her that had existed before the revelation, the warmth that had been real even when the intent underneath it wasn't.

He thought about Sir Elliot's sword, and the benefactor who'd sent it, and the way the blade had been there exactly when he needed it. He would carry the sword for twenty years before he understood the full shape of that particular arrangement. He would meet the benefactor eventually, in a city that pulled old things to it the way London pulled darkness, and he would walk onto an investigation already in progress and look at the immortal running it and feel the click of something falling into place that had been loose for two decades.

He would not mention the sword first. He would wait to see if the benefactor mentioned it.

He was less open than he had been at twenty-six. He was, in most of the ways that mattered, more himself.

London had done that.
