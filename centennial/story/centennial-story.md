# New York

*The story of Aluccard, the last of the Velkynai, and the night the city that never sleeps found out what was hunting in it.*

---

## Prologue: The Last of a Kind

He had outlived languages.

That was the thought Aluccard returned to sometimes, in the hours before the city woke up — that he had lived long enough to watch the tongues of entire civilizations go silent, to watch their words go from common speech to scholarly study to carved stone nobody could read anymore. He had outlived empires, fashions, philosophies, three distinct theories of what the sun was. He had outlived almost everyone he had ever cared about, which was a list that had grown shorter and shorter across the centuries until it fit inside his chest like a stone.

He ran a technology company now. He sat in a glass building in Manhattan and made decisions about software and acquisitions and quarterly projections, and did it well enough that nobody looked too closely at the CEO who hadn't visibly aged since the company's founding and whose personnel file listed a date of birth that the HR system kept flagging as a data entry error.

He had spent the last few centuries collecting things. Knowledge, mostly. Resources. Whatever scraps of the old world's magic were still lying around in the twenty-first century, he found and secured and catalogued, because he was the last of the Velkynai and it fell to him by default and because the alternative was leaving it for someone less careful.

He had not expected to be afraid. Not at this age. Not after everything.

But something was killing immortals in New York City, and Aluccard could feel, for the first time in a very long time, what mortality had felt like when he still had it.

## Chapter One: Lucan

The body was in a brownstone in Brooklyn.

Leon had found it first, which was how most of Aluccard's mornings began — Leon, already awake, already across town, already three steps into something that was going to require Aluccard to put on a suit and explain himself to several different kinds of people who wouldn't believe the truth. His apprentice was mortal and young and had been pushed past the ordinary limits of both, which meant he moved through the world with an urgency that Aluccard sometimes found exhausting and sometimes found the only thing bearable about the century they were living in.

"It's Lucan," Leon said on the phone. Nothing else.

Aluccard got there in fifteen minutes.

Lucan was one of those immortals who had never made much of himself in the grand sense — no empires, no legendary deeds, no name in the books. He had been a prince once, in a time so long ago that the kingdom didn't have a surviving record. He had lost his family and his closest companion over the years the way immortals always lost people, one by one, across too many decades for any of it to feel bearable. And still he had kept going. That was the thing about Lucan that Aluccard had always, privately, respected: the man simply liked being alive, and held onto it without pretense or particular reason, just the plain stubborn fact of it.

The body told him very little about who had done this.

Among Lucan's belongings, tucked under the bed, was a suit of armor — old, well-maintained, not the kind of thing you kept if you weren't prepared to use it. Aluccard stood there holding it for a long moment.

"He was ready," Leon said quietly.

"He was," Aluccard said. He set the armor down carefully, the way you set down something that deserves respect even after the person who owned it is gone.

The anger came later, on the drive back. Not grief exactly — grief he was practiced at, had learned its shape from the inside over centuries. This was something else. Simpler and colder. Someone had killed a quiet man who liked being alive, and Aluccard intended to find out who.

## Chapter Two: The Circle

The first call he made was to Death.

This was not as dramatic as it sounded. Death ran a jazz bar in the West Village called *The Last Measure*, wore expensive suits, and had been slowly working through an existential crisis about the nature of mortality for the better part of three hundred years. He had retired from active reaping some time ago, though the retirement was complicated by the fact that he could still feel the weight of souls leaving the world — the heavy ones especially.

"Immortal souls," Death said, without looking up from the glass he was polishing, "fall differently than mortal ones. You can feel the extra weight."

"How many?" Aluccard asked.

"More than you'd expect. Less than you'd hope." He set the glass down. "I'm not going to help you investigate this."

"I know," Aluccard said. "I just needed to know you'd noticed."

Death looked at him for a moment with the particular expression of someone who has been around long enough to recognize when they're being handled. "I've noticed," he said, finally. "I always notice. That's what makes retirement complicated."

The second call was to Arthur, which was harder than calling Death had been, because Arthur was a ghost from another age and conversations with him still cost something that Aluccard couldn't name precisely.

He appeared at the window that night, visible because it was dark. He looked exactly as he always had. He said he'd been trying to find Aluccard for weeks, that he'd felt something off in the city, something pulling at the boundary between the living and the dead in ways that shouldn't be happening.

"I know," Aluccard said again. "That's why I called."

"You didn't call," Arthur said. "I appeared."

"Effectively the same thing."

Arthur stayed. He had always stayed.

The third call was to Hythm, which went badly.

Hythm had given Aluccard his crown years ago — had sealed his memories away inside it because he could no longer carry the weight of having lived that long, and had decided to be mortal about the whole thing. Aluccard had agreed to hold onto it, which had seemed, at the time, like the compassionate choice.

He found Hythm in a back room of a bar in Queens, deep into a losing streak he'd been sustaining for what looked like several weeks. The man he saw was not the king he had once marched beside. He was a person who had decided that forgetting was the same as healing and was discovering, the hard way, that it wasn't.

Aluccard offered him the crown.

Hythm told him to leave.

"You may still be a target," Aluccard said. "Whether you remember anything or not."

"Then I'll die not remembering," Hythm said, without looking up from the table. "That's how mortals do it. It's fine."

Aluccard left the crown on the bar anyway, where Hythm could ignore it, which he did.

He would worry about Hythm later. He had several other things to worry about first.

## Chapter Three: Suspects

The list wrote itself.

Paradigm was the obvious name. God of technology, born sometime in the industrial revolution and fed by every subsequent century's hunger for new machinery, new systems, new ways of making the old world obsolete. He and Aluccard were already in the middle of a hostile takeover that had been going on long enough to have developed its own mythology in the financial press. If something was culling old power from the world to make room for new power, Paradigm had motive that basically wrote his name in capital letters across the top of any reasonable suspect list.

Father Elias was the less obvious name. He had been a priest once, in an age when being a priest meant something different than it did now, and had founded something he called a faith and Aluccard called a cult, sustained by dark magic and a medallion around his neck that allegedly contained the heart of a dead god. His immortality was intact. His influence was considerably less so — faith had been losing to technology and money for decades, and Elias had the specific bitterness of a man who had once been extremely powerful and had watched that power be replaced by things he didn't understand and couldn't control.

He had set disciples to follow Aluccard. Aluccard had noticed them after the second day and had found the surveillance both insulting and vaguely useful. At least if Elias's people were watching him, he had some sense of where they were.

And then there was Patchwork, which didn't fit.

Patchwork was a problem of a different kind — a soldier from an ancient era who had died thousands of years ago and had no business being in the twenty-first century at all, and yet kept showing up in places associated with the immortal deaths, always just on the edge of the frame, never quite caught. He had a fondness for toxic gas that hadn't changed across the centuries, and the new tools available to him seemed to delight him in the way that new toys delight people who were never safe to begin with.

Aluccard wrote him off as a distraction initially. Then he noticed the pattern. Patchwork wasn't incidental to the deaths. He was there at all of them. Which meant someone had brought him back — put him in this era, in this city, for a reason — and that someone was the problem Patchwork was obscuring.

## Chapter Four: Gareth and Aetheris

The gunslinger showed up at Lucan's scene three days after the body had been removed.

He had one arm — the other was a mechanical replacement that looked expensive and clearly wasn't factory standard — and the complete confidence of someone who had learned that the most effective way to handle being immediately distrusted was to simply not stop moving. He introduced himself to Aluccard and Leon by accusing them of the murder, reconsidered when Aluccard stared at him without blinking for approximately fifteen seconds, and then offered to join the investigation on the grounds that he'd been working the immortal deaths circuit from the outside for two weeks and had information that might be useful.

Aluccard said no.

Gareth came along anyway, which was annoying in the specific way that useful people who make bad first impressions are annoying.

He had, as it turned out, useful information. He also had a name that meant something to Aetheris.

Aetheris was the last person Aluccard wanted to involve. He had found her — as he had found many of the city's old immortals — not through magic or tracking but through the mundane fact that New York attracted a remarkable number of beings who had been alive long enough to need to be somewhere nobody asked too many questions, and Manhattan in particular seemed to have a gravity to it that pulled the ancient and the tired. Aetheris had been the being who maintained the balance of the world once, powerful enough to shift any conflict she entered. Now she worked a nine-to-five and watched television in the evenings and had said, politely and firmly, that she wanted no part of whatever Aluccard was putting together.

He brought Gareth with him the second time.

She looked at the gunslinger for a long moment. Then she said that he looked like someone she had fought beside thousands of years ago. Not identical — family resemblance, across generations so long the generations had become geological. Something in the posture, or the eyes, or whatever quality it was that made a hero recognizable to someone old enough to have seen what heroism actually looked like in the original.

"Just like that?" Aluccard said.

"Just like that," Aetheris said, and went to get her sword.

## Chapter Five: Elias and Andromedus

The confrontation with Father Elias happened in a church that had been deconsecrated for forty years and was therefore, in Elias's reckoning, the only appropriate kind of place to hold a meeting.

They circled each other for a while, the way two people do when they are genuinely uncertain whether the other person is responsible for a series of murders and also cannot afford to resolve that uncertainty violently. Elias had the bearing of a man who had once commanded rooms and was still expecting them to obey, wearing it over the more recent truth of a man whose congregation had slowly discovered that technology could give them the same feeling of meaning and transcendence at a fraction of the spiritual overhead.

He knew about Lucan. He knew about the others. He was angry, in the specific way that someone is angry when they hate the thing that killed somebody but would have been fine with it happening to everyone else.

"I didn't kill them," Elias said.

"I know," Aluccard said.

This surprised him. "Then why —"

"Because you know who did," Aluccard said, "or you know what. And you'd like it found."

The alliance that formed was cold and transactional and both of them knew exactly what it was. Elias offered money and connections in exchange for the killer being brought to him, for whatever private reckoning he had decided he was owed. Aluccard accepted on the grounds that Elias's connections were extensive and his own patience for the investigation's pace was wearing thin.

Andromedus found him the following morning.

The blue-skinned man had been trying to get Aluccard's attention for weeks — running the invisible infrastructure of supernatural concealment in the city, keeping the bodies of dead immortals out of the wrong government labs, holding the seams of the ordinary world together at the places where it kept threatening to come apart. He was one of the oldest things alive and wore it as lightly as a good suit, which Aluccard found either impressive or suspect depending on his mood.

"You've been avoiding me," Andromedus said.

"I've been busy."

"You've been avoiding me," Andromedus said again, pleasantly.

Aluccard considered the blue man and the long history of his own reluctance and the fact that every week he spent working around him was a week the investigation didn't progress.

"Tell me what you know," he said.

Andromedus had quite a lot to tell him.

## Chapter Six: Leon

It was Leon who found the pattern in the deaths.

This was, in retrospect, predictable — Leon was mortal and had therefore never developed the habit that long-lived beings tend to develop of thinking in centuries, which made him sharper at the granular level, better at noticing the things that happened in weeks and days. He had been cross-referencing the locations of the deaths, the timing, the manner, and the one thing Patchwork kept leaving behind — not a signature, exactly, more like a method, a way of approaching something that was consistent because the man doing it had been doing it the same way for thousands of years.

The deaths were a funnel.

Patchwork wasn't killing immortals because someone wanted them dead. He was killing them because someone needed the weight of their souls moved in a particular direction, in a particular sequence, to accomplish something specific — and that something was happening in whatever passed for the machinery of the underworld when you were looking at it from the outside.

Dreyfus, Leon said quietly, showing Aluccard the pattern on his laptop. Ruler of the underworld. Former king. He had been using Minodas — the venture capitalist, the deal-maker, the soul-collector — to funnel the living into his influence for years, normal slow work. This was something different, something faster and more violent. The kind of move you made when you were tired of patience.

"He wants out," Aluccard said.

"He wants back in," Leon said. "There's a difference."

There was a difference. Back in was worse.

## Chapter Seven: Dreyfus

They found out he could possess people when Leon started behaving unlike himself.

It lasted forty minutes — enough for Dreyfus to make clear that he knew who they all were, what they were doing, and that his opinion of the investigation was somewhere between contempt and amusement. He appeared, in Leon's voice and with Leon's face doing things Leon's face would not do, to be disgusted by Aluccard's existence in the way that very old evil tends to be disgusted by things that persist without its permission.

He said Drexl's name with a specific revulsion that made Aluccard file that away for later.

When it passed, Leon sat down very carefully and looked at his hands for a while and then said he was fine, he just needed a minute. Aluccard gave him five. Then they went back to work.

Dreyfus could not act in the mortal world directly. He needed Patchwork for the killings, Minodas for the soul-collection, the bodies of compromised mortals for communication. He was enormously powerful inside his domain and mostly theoretical outside it. The gap between those two things was the only vulnerability they had.

Kleon had been watching the investigation from a distance — the time-displaced king of the vigilante circuit, who ran his own crusade against the city's corporate sins and had more than once crossed Aluccard's path in ways that had not ended with either of them happy. He would not work with Aluccard. The grudge was old and genuine and neither of them had the energy to perform a reconciliation they didn't mean.

But he sent information. Left it in places where Leon would find it, through channels that maintained the fiction of non-collaboration. Aluccard acknowledged this exactly once, to Leon, in private.

"He's helping," Leon said.

"He's leaving useful things where we can find them," Aluccard said. "That's not the same as helping."

"It's the same as helping," Leon said.

## Chapter Eight: The Resurrection

He had not told anyone he'd done it until Drexl showed up at his door.

That was a problem with Drexl Voss — he had always shown up, from one world into another, at the moments when his presence was simultaneously the most necessary and the most complicated. Aluccard had brought him back through a combination of things he had collected over centuries: old magic, salvaged technology, knowledge that had no business being applied to this particular purpose. He had done it because the investigation was moving toward a confrontation he couldn't be certain he survived, and because some part of him — the part that had learned to carry loss without being broken by it — had simply not been ready to carry Drexl Voss permanently.

The knight was not grateful.

"You shouldn't have done this," Drexl said. He looked the same as he always had, disfigured in the same places, carrying himself with the same selfless directness that had made him who he was across two worlds.

"Probably not," Aluccard said.

"You bent the rules of life and death because you didn't want to be without me."

"The investigation needed —"

"Aluccard."

He stopped.

"You bent the rules of life and death," Drexl said again, quietly, "because you didn't want to be without me. That's what happened. I'm not angry about it. But I need you to say it."

Aluccard said it. It cost him something, saying it plainly, which was the point.

Drexl stayed. He joined the investigation with the same uncomplicated commitment he had always brought to causes that deserved it and several that hadn't, and he did not bring it up again. But he watched Aluccard in a way he hadn't before — with the specific attention of someone who has discovered that an old friend is more breakable than they appeared, and has decided to keep track.

## Chapter Nine: Ahere

He came on a Tuesday, which was an appropriate day for things that had no business happening.

Aluccard was crossing the park when he felt it — not saw, felt, the specific prickling of a presence he had last felt at the end of a fight on a forest road, thousands of years and several lifetimes ago. He stopped walking. He looked at the man standing fifty yards away who was looking at him with the particular focus of someone who has been waiting for this for a very long time.

Ahere.

He was wearing different clothes and carrying different weapons and he had the look of someone who had spent the intervening centuries being furious, which was a substantial amount of time to sustain any single emotion. The modern weapons were a touch that Aluccard found, against his better judgment, almost impressive.

"How," Aluccard said.

"Does it matter," Ahere said.

It didn't, really, in the moment. In the moment what mattered was that his former mentor — the man he had killed on a road because a king had sent him — was standing in a New York City park in the twenty-first century with the clear intention of finishing something that Aluccard had started by surviving.

He didn't want to fight him.

He had not wanted to fight him the first time either, but the first time there had been a road and a forest and the simple facts of what had been asked of him. This time there was a city going about its morning around them, and Aluccard was tired in the way that living forever eventually makes you tired, and Ahere was there because Aluccard had killed him and he had come back and there was no version of this conversation that ended with a handshake.

They fought, and it was harder than it had been the first time, because everything was different and also nothing was.

Aluccard won. He was better. He had always been better, and the centuries had widened the gap rather than closing it. He stood over his former mentor at the end and felt exactly what he had felt on the road in Aridel: not clean.

Ahere looked up at him with the expression of someone who has run a very long distance toward something and is only now, arriving, unsure whether it was the right direction.

"I taught you well," he said, which was not what Aluccard had expected.

"You did," Aluccard said. "I'm sorry."

"You were always going to be better than me," Ahere said. "I knew it the moment you got up off the floor." Something shifted in his face, just slightly. "I hated you for it. Took a very long time to figure out that wasn't your fault."

He didn't say anything after that.

Aluccard sat on a park bench for a while and watched the city move around him and thought about the weight of a very long life and whether the fact that you could carry it meant you were supposed to.

He decided that was a question for later. There was still Dreyfus.

## Chapter Ten: The End of It

The confrontation with Dreyfus was not the kind of fight that happens in a single place.

It happened in the mortal world through the vessels Dreyfus used — Patchwork first, run to ground in the industrial corridors of Brooklyn where he'd been operating, disoriented by the twenty-first century in ways that made him dangerous and also, ultimately, manageable. Gareth and Aetheris handled Patchwork, which was the right allocation. Aetheris moved like someone who had once maintained the balance of the entire world and still had the muscle memory. Gareth moved like someone who had lost an arm and learned to fight better anyway.

Minodas was Aluccard's.

The venture capitalist met him in an office building with floor-to-ceiling windows and the specific confidence of something that had been offering ruinous deals for centuries and had rarely been told no. Aluccard told him no in a way that didn't require words.

Without Patchwork and Minodas, Dreyfus's reach into the mortal world collapsed into a single point — the body he'd been cycling through, one compromised vessel after another, trying to hold his position. Drexl found the vessel. Arthur, visible in the darkness between the building's lights, kept him contained. Leon coordinated everything from a screen in a car two blocks away with the calm focus of a man who had decided some time ago that the work was worth doing and had not revisited the decision since.

They didn't destroy Dreyfus. You couldn't, exactly. What they did was fold him back, closing the door he'd spent decades prying open, returning him to the domain he'd been trying to escape. Father Elias arrived for the reckoning he'd been promised and found something considerably less satisfying than he'd expected — a ruler returned to his kingdom rather than a power delivered helpless into his hands. He was displeased. Aluccard told him that was as close to justice as this particular situation allowed, and left before the priest could form a full objection.

The city did not notice. That was Andromedus's work — had been, throughout, the invisible infrastructure of a being who had decided his purpose was keeping ordinary life ordinary, one suppressed supernatural incident at a time. Aluccard acknowledged this on the way out, which was as close as he came to gratitude.

"You're welcome," Andromedus said pleasantly. "And the next time I try to approach you about a collaboration —"

"I'll take the meeting," Aluccard said. "Probably."

## Epilogue: The Morning After

Leon found him on the roof of the building at five in the morning.

He was looking at the city the way you look at something when you're trying to decide whether you still recognize it, or whether it's simply changed again while you were occupied with keeping it from being destroyed. This had happened enough times across Aluccard's life that he'd stopped being surprised by it. He was not always sure whether that was wisdom or damage.

"Hythm took the crown," Leon said.

Aluccard looked at him.

"Went back for it this morning. I checked." Leon sat down on the edge of the roof beside him, which was the kind of thing mortals could do without it meaning anything in particular. "He called me. Said he didn't open it. Just said he wanted it somewhere he could find it."

"That's not the same as wanting it back."

"No," Leon said. "But it's closer."

Below them the city was doing what it always did at five in the morning, which was the same thing it did at every other hour except slightly quieter and slightly more honest. Aluccard had lived in a lot of cities across a lot of centuries and none of them had ever managed to be as simultaneously exhausting and necessary as this one, which was either its greatest quality or its most effective trap.

He thought about Lucan's armor, folded under a bed, ready. About Drexl watching him with the specific attention of someone who had noticed, finally, that his old friend was not as steady as he looked. About Ahere saying *I knew it the moment you got up off the floor* with something in his voice that might, if you were generous, have been pride.

He thought about how long he had been alive and how much of that life had been spent saying goodbye to people, and whether the fact that he was still standing was the point of the story or just a detail of it.

He thought: there are people in this city who are targets simply because they have lived long enough to matter.

He thought: someone has to stay.

Below them, the city brightened by a degree as the sun found its angle. Leon didn't say anything. Aluccard didn't say anything. The morning came in the way mornings come in New York — without asking, without apology, all at once.

He got up. He had a company to run.
