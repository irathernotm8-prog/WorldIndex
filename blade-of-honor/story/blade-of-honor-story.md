# Blade of Honor

*The story of Aluccard, of two men named Drexl, and of a king who forgot what he stood for.*

---

## Prologue: The State of the World

Aridel was a kingdom that had once meant something.

That was the trouble with it — you could still see the shape of what it had been in the architecture of its older streets, in the way certain elders spoke about Edwin before they caught themselves and changed the subject. Something had happened here, or rather something had been happening, slowly enough that no single person could point to the day it started. The roads were still maintained. The taxes were collected and mostly spent on something defensible. But the warmth was gone, replaced by the particular chill of a place governed by a man who had stopped believing in what he was doing and had not yet found anything to replace it with.

Edwin still sat the throne. Edwin still gave the speeches. And somewhere, in the mountains to the north, a fire lord named Kruxer was burning his way toward the kingdom's borders, not for conquest — not for anything so rational as that — but because he wanted to scorch everything alive, and Aridel simply happened to be in his path.

Most kings would have raised an army.

Edwin sent out word that he was looking for heroes.

## Chapter One: Two Men, One Road

Aluccard had been thrown out of enough places to know that it wasn't always his fault.

His skin was too pale, his frame too lean, his eyes the wrong color for the village he'd been born in and every village since. He carried himself carefully, the way people who have learned that they make others uncomfortable tend to do — quietly, with his hands visible, taking up as little space as possible. He was not meek. He had a very clear sense of right and wrong and a persistent inability to ignore either. That combination had gotten him into fights he couldn't always win and out of towns he hadn't intended to leave.

He met Drexl in a tavern on the eastern road, which was not a promising beginning for either of them.

The mercenary was loud in the way that people who've never had to be quiet are loud — not obnoxiously, just completely, filling whatever room he occupied with the fact of his presence. Broad, dark, scarred in exactly the pattern you'd expect on someone who'd been fighting professionally since he was old enough to hold a weapon. He had a name that carried weight in certain circles, which he'd figured out early and had never stopped using to his advantage.

They came to blows over something Aluccard had said about the people at the next table.

Drexl won. It wasn't close.

Aluccard got up off the floor, wiped his mouth, and asked if Drexl would teach him.

Drexl laughed for a long time. Then he said yes, because a decoy could be useful, and because something about the pale kid getting up off the floor after a beating like that had caught his attention in a way he wasn't going to examine too closely.

## Chapter Two: Edwin's Cause

Word of Edwin's gold — and Edwin's purpose — reached them three months later.

Drexl heard money. Aluccard heard something else: a king who wanted to unite the kingdom, to drive back Kruxer's forces, to govern a country that served its people rather than the other way around. He'd heard versions of this speech before and had usually been skeptical. Edwin's version landed differently. There was something in the way the king said it — not the practiced cadence of a man who'd given the speech many times, but the specific, slightly urgent quality of a man who still believed the words he was saying. 

Aluccard paid attention to that.

"He's paying well either way," Drexl said, which was also true.

They found the others on the road — Arthur, who moved too quietly for a man in full armor and never removed his helmet; Baine, who had no name for himself except the one they gave him, and who muttered things under his breath that nobody could quite make out; Florkil, who had come from the northern territories specifically to die in a worthy fight and had no particular preference about the cause that occasioned it. They made an unlikely group. They made it work, the way people with nothing in common sometimes do when the thing in front of them is large enough to require all of them.

Edwin met with them once, early on. He shook Aluccard's hand and looked him in the eye and said that what they were doing mattered. Aluccard believed him.

He would remember that handshake for a long time afterward.

---

## Chapter Three: The Road to Kruxer

The march north was long and the losses came before they reached Kruxer's walls.

Baine fought until the last days of the approach, still whispering his fragment of a purpose, still moving forward on whatever it was that compelled him. He never made it to the Last Bastion. Syloth — Kruxer's black knight, immovable at the gate — saw to that. The fight with Syloth cost them more than Baine. It cost them the version of themselves that had started the journey thinking they might all make it through.

Florkil got his worthy death in the assault on the walls. He met it with something close to gratitude, which Aluccard found harder to watch than the grief would have been.

Arthur fell to Syloth as well — or rather, the armor fell, which turned out to be the same thing. What had held Arthur to the world was the tether the armor represented, and Syloth broke it. They didn't find a body. Stories of a ghost following Edwin began circulating shortly afterward, first among the castle servants and then further, the way those stories go.

Inside the walls, Kruxer waited.

Drexl went for him.

---

## Chapter Four: The Fire and the Mercenary

The fight between Drexl and Kruxer happened in a part of the castle that was already coming down around them.

Aluccard had fought his way through to the chamber and arrived in time to see how it was going — which was badly, in the specific way that fights go badly when one of the combatants has magic and the other has only skill and the absolute refusal to stop. Drexl was the finest fighter Aluccard had ever seen. Kruxer was something else. The fire came off him in waves, and Drexl's armor was black in places it hadn't been before.

He was winning anyway, in the sense that he was still standing and Kruxer was not winning, which was the most you could say.

What happened next was so fast that Aluccard spent months afterward trying to understand it.

Kruxer's fire, redirected, came at Aluccard — a final act, not strategy exactly, more like a last reflex from something that had always wanted to destroy everything in reach. Drexl stepped into it. The exchange that followed lasted seconds. When it was over, Kruxer was dead, and so was Drexl.

The mercenary had enough time to look at Aluccard before he went. He didn't say anything. He looked, for the first time, like he understood something he hadn't understood before. Then he was gone.

Aluccard sat in the ash and the ruin for a long time. He had not expected to grieve a man who'd beaten him half-senseless in a tavern and called him a decoy for three months. He grieved him anyway, thoroughly and without reservation, which was the only way Aluccard had ever done anything.

---

## Chapter Five: The Portal

The portal appeared on the road back.

That was the simplest way to describe it — it appeared, which was not something roads normally did, and out of it stepped a man who was not from this world in any way that could be mistaken. He carried himself like someone who had seen catastrophe and had not been broken by it, exactly, but had been permanently changed in a way that showed.

His name was Drexl Voss. He said it simply, without explanation, watching Aluccard's face.

The name hit like a stone into water. Aluccard didn't speak for a moment. Then he said: "That's not a name I expected to hear again."

"I know," Drexl Voss said. "I'm sorry."

They walked for a while in silence, which is sometimes the only appropriate response to a world that does that kind of thing.

Drexl Voss had come from a place where he'd served as a knight of the Hidden Holy Order, holding back something he called the abyss. He'd failed. His world had ended. The portal had brought him here, and he did not fully understand why, except that when he'd come through it and seen Aluccard standing alone on the road, something in him had recognized a purpose he thought he'd lost.

His face was disfigured from the ordeal. He didn't talk about it directly. He carried a sword he called Thunder-Guard, which had its own properties that he also didn't talk about directly, having come from a tradition where the work was more important than the explaining of it.

Their bond built differently than Aluccard's bond with the first Drexl had — without the friction, without the imbalance of student and reluctant teacher. They were two people who had each lost something fundamental and had found, unexpectedly, that the loss had made them compatible in a way that ease never quite does.

They went to find Edwin. Edwin sent them south.

---

## Chapter Six: Victor

Victor's territory was flat and grey and covered with castles he was in the middle of building, using labor that had not agreed to be labor. He never removed his armor. He operated entirely without cruelty, which made him, in some ways, more unsettling than Kruxer had been — there was no malice in him, just an absolute removal from the fact that the people around him were people. They were labor. The castles were for Vornath, whoever or whatever Vornath was. Victor served Vornath the way weather serves nobody, simply following the direction it had been given.

The battle was brutal in the way that battles against someone who doesn't feel fear tend to be.

Drexl Voss died in it.

Victor killed him, in an exchange that lasted longer than it should have and ended the way things end when one side is fighting for something and the other side simply won't stop. Aluccard watched it happen and did not have time to stop it and spent the rest of that fight killing Victor with the specific, cold efficiency of someone who has moved past grief into something quieter and more complete.

He sat with Drexl Voss for longer than the situation required.

He had said goodbye to two men named Drexl now, which was the kind of thing that shouldn't happen to anyone once. He sat there until the light changed, and then he got up and walked back north to tell Edwin it was done.

---

## Chapter Seven: What Edwin Had Become

The kingdom was not what it had been.

This was obvious from the road in. It was not obvious in any single concrete way — the walls were standing, the guards were present, the banners displayed the right colors. It was obvious in the same way it had been obvious when Aluccard first arrived: in the temperature of the place, the way people didn't quite meet each other's eyes, the specific quiet of a population that has learned that speaking up costs more than it returns.

Edwin received him in the throne room.

Aluccard remembered the man who had shaken his hand and looked him in the eye and said that what they were doing mattered. He looked at the man on the throne and tried to find that king inside this one and could not locate him. It wasn't that Edwin had become cruel. It was that he had become careful, in the way that erodes everything generous and leaves only the structure behind. The words were right. The timing was right. Nothing was right.

He told the king what had been accomplished.

Edwin nodded, said the appropriate things, offered the appropriate reward.

Aluccard took the reward and left.

---

## Chapter Eight: Ahere

He was three days out from the capital when Ahere found him.

He had wondered if this would happen. He hadn't wondered long — it was the kind of thing you either think about until it becomes an obsession or you file away in a part of your mind marked *deal with it when it arrives* and move on. Aluccard had always been better at the second approach.

Ahere had been his mentor, once, before Drexl. Before the pale skin and the wrong-colored eyes had found a different teacher. Ahere had taught him things that were useful and some that Aluccard had decided not to keep. They had parted badly, the way you part with someone whose methods you've outgrown but whose face is still the face of a time when you were young enough to believe that the person teaching you was entirely worth believing in.

The fact that Edwin had sent him was the most honest thing Edwin had communicated in some time. He hadn't said it. Edwin never would have said it directly. But he had sent Ahere, and Ahere was not the kind of errand you sent when you wanted to give a man a pension and a quiet retirement.

They stood on a road in a forest, in the particular stillness of a confrontation that both sides had known was coming.

"You don't have to do this," Aluccard said.

Ahere smiled. It was not a kind smile; it had never been a kind smile. "He pays well," he said. "You know how it is."

Aluccard did know. That was the problem.

The fight was not long. Aluccard had grown past his teacher years before they met on this road — grown past him in ways that the Velkynai bite had accelerated and that Drexl and Drexl Voss had refined. He won cleanly, which did not make it feel clean.

He stood over Ahere at the end and thought about what it meant that the king he had followed had hired the assassin who had once taught him to be worth killing. He thought about the first Drexl, who had died for him in the ash of Kruxer's castle and would never have understood why. He thought about Drexl Voss, who had crossed worlds to find a purpose and had found it and had spent it entirely, which was perhaps the only way to spend a purpose that was ever really worth anything.

He did not go back to Edwin.

---

## Epilogue: The Cause Continues

The cause did not end with him leaving.

That was the thing about it — about all of it. The people Edwin had once inspired still carried what he'd said to them in the early days, before the careful erosion, before the temperature of the kingdom changed. They built things with it. Small things, mostly, and some not so small. A village council that survived two bad winters because of decisions made by people who had heard Edwin's early speeches and taken them more seriously than Edwin eventually did. A road. A law, passed quietly, that protected people who looked the way Aluccard looked, pale skin and wrong-colored eyes and all.

Edwin was still on the throne.

Aluccard walked north, and then east, and then wherever the road went next, changed in ways he was still learning to name and carrying everyone he'd lost the only way any of them get carried — forward, imperfectly, in the direction of whatever came next.

Somewhere behind him, a ghost moved quietly through the corridors of a castle, keeping watch over a king who had stopped deserving it and couldn't quite bring himself to leave.

Arthur had always been loyal. That had been his problem and his gift both, and now it was simply the thing that remained when everything else had been stripped away.

The cause outlasted the man who named it.

That was, in the end, the most honest thing you could say about Edwin — and the most honest thing you could say about all of it.
