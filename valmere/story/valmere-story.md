# Valmere

*The story of a nation that appeared out of nowhere, a man sent to find its flaws, and what he found instead.*

---

## Prologue: The Address

The broadcast happened on a Tuesday morning in April 2035, which was an unremarkable choice of day for the most remarkable thing that had happened to the international order in living memory.

Van Burnic appeared on every major network simultaneously, which was itself a statement — not a leak, not a scheduled press conference, but a coordinated transmission that reached every corner of the world at the same moment, as if Valmere had been waiting for exactly the right second to exist and had chosen this one. He was standing in front of a city that nobody had photographs of, against a skyline of spires and waterways that looked like it had been built over centuries by people who understood that beauty was as important as function and had refused to choose between them.

He was kind in his address, which caught everyone off guard. Heads of state, when introducing themselves to the world, tend toward either the grandiose or the defensive. Van Burnic was neither. He said: *We are here. We are welcoming to all peoples. We do not tolerate aggression.* He said it the way someone says something they have believed for a long time and have finally found the right moment to say aloud.

He made clear that he was head of the nation not by any special means — he had been selected by the Council of Valmere, the governing body of an island nation that had spent decades preparing to introduce itself and had apparently spent that time well. He extended an invitation to every country to send diplomatic envoys. He said Valmere looked forward to building relationships.

Then, because he understood that the first thing anyone would worry about, he released twelve peer-reviewed scientific studies confirming that the island nation's construction in the mid-Atlantic had not affected any weather patterns, any oceanic currents, or any marine ecosystems within a thousand-mile radius. He released them before anyone asked. He knew they would ask.

The world spent approximately seventy-two hours in a state of collective stunned silence, and then the questions began.

America was the loudest skeptic. America was almost always the loudest skeptic when things appeared that it hadn't invented. It sent its best people to ask its hardest questions. Van Burnic, having anticipated this, personally invited the American envoy to come and see for themselves.

He was, by all accounts, very good at this.

## Chapter One: Drake Arrives

Drake had been in intelligence long enough to know that the most useful thing a new posting could do for your career was to find a problem nobody else had found. Valmere was the biggest problem nobody had found in decades — a technologically advanced island nation that had apparently existed for some time before revealing itself, which meant it had been deliberately concealed, which meant someone knew it was there and had agreed to look away, which meant there was a deal, and deals had terms, and terms could be exploited.

This was the kind of thinking that made Drake good at his job.

He landed at Valmere's central port on a morning in June 2035 with a diplomatic credential and a secondary objective that his credential did not mention. The port was extraordinary — not in the way that wealth tends to be extraordinary, which is usually about scale and exclusion, but in the way that genuine civic planning tends to be extraordinary, which is about the sense that someone has thought carefully about what a space is for and made it exactly that. Ships moved through channels between the city's concentric rings. Water turbines turned quietly beneath the surface. Everything was white and blue-green and very clean.

Almira met him at the port.

She was the UN Ambassador for Valmere and she moved through the space with the ease of someone who had helped design what was being shown, which she had. She shook his hand and looked at him with the direct, measuring quality of someone who had been assessed by enough diplomats to know what an assessment felt like and was deciding to simply acknowledge it rather than perform unconcern.

"You're not just here to observe," she said, as they walked.

"I'm here on behalf of my government," Drake said.

"Those aren't mutually exclusive," Almira said pleasantly, and showed him the waterways.

---

## Chapter Two: The City

Valmere was three concentric rings around a central island, connected by bridges and divided by water channels that were also the city's power source and its water supply. The engineering was remarkable and not secret — Valmere published its infrastructure designs openly, which Drake filed as either genuine confidence or an extremely sophisticated form of misdirection and couldn't immediately determine which.

The architecture was the kind that required decades of sustained intention — not just money, but a coherent vision maintained across long enough that every building reflected the same set of decisions about what a city should feel like. Drake had been to a lot of cities. He had never been to one that felt this *decided*.

Almira showed him the turbines, the water processing, the distributed energy grid. She showed him the schools, which were multilingual by design. She showed him the markets, which used Valmerie crowns but accepted thirty other currencies without complaint. She answered every question he asked with the directness of someone who has nothing to hide about the things they're choosing to show you.

He kept notes. He kept separate notes. He was looking for the gap between what was shown and what was there, which was his job, and the gap was very small, which made him either impressed or more suspicious depending on his mood.

He met Van Burnic on his fourth day.

---

## Chapter Three: Van Burnic

The leader of Valmere received him in a room that looked out over the central waterway, with the kind of view that a person with absolute control over a nation's architecture might arrange for themselves and that Van Burnic had apparently not arranged at all — his office was functional and modest, which was either genuine indifference to optics or the most sophisticated optic of all.

He shook Drake's hand and looked at him, and Drake had the specific experience of being looked at by someone who was genuinely interested in what they were looking at, which was rarer than it should have been in his line of work.

They talked for three hours.

Drake had been briefed extensively on Van Burnic. He had read every interview, every public statement, every analysis his department had produced. He was prepared for the charisma, which every brief had noted. He was not prepared for the quality of the man's actual attention — the way he asked questions that suggested he had thought carefully about what he didn't know and wanted to fix that, specifically, through the conversation they were having right now.

Van Burnic asked Drake about America. Not its foreign policy, not its military, but about what it was actually like to live there, what it was like to believe in it, what it asked of the people who loved it and whether the asking was worth it. He asked the way someone asks who has spent a long time thinking about nationhood — about what it means for a country to earn the loyalty of the people inside it rather than simply assert a claim to it.

Drake found himself answering honestly, which was not what he had intended.

He left the meeting with the unsettling sense that he had been assessed at least as thoroughly as he had done any assessing, and that Van Burnic had found what he was looking for and was neither surprised nor troubled by it.

He went back to his room and wrote two reports — one for his government, and one that he didn't send to anyone.

---

## Chapter Four: Clive

Drake noticed Clive on his sixth day, which was two days longer than it should have taken.

The telepath moved through Valmere with the understated efficiency of someone whose job was to not be noticed while being absolutely aware of everything. Drake had been in intelligence long enough to recognise the quality of sustained attention even when it wasn't pointed at him, and Clive's attention had a specific texture — not surveillance exactly, more like a very broad and very fine net, maintained permanently, that registered anything that moved through it with intent to harm.

He raised this with Almira, carefully.

She was quiet for a moment. "Clive is part of our border security," she said. "His specific capabilities are not public knowledge."

"I understand that," Drake said. "I'm asking because I'm wondering what he found in me when I arrived."

Almira looked at him with the specific expression of someone deciding how much honesty the moment required. "He found a man with a secondary objective," she said. "He also found that the objective was professional rather than personal, and that you were genuinely uncertain about what you were going to do with what you found."

"And you let me in anyway."

"Van Burnic specifically requested that we did," Almira said. "He finds genuinely uncertain people more useful than certain ones."

Drake thought about this for a long time.

The question that Clive's existence raised — that a country built on freedom used a telepath to screen its arrivals — was one that Drake raised with Van Burnic directly, in their second meeting. Van Burnic received the question without defensiveness.

"It's a legitimate concern," he said. "Every security measure is a question about what you're willing to do to protect what you have. We chose this one because it's precise and because Clive is disciplined about the distinction between threat and difference. We live with the ethical discomfort of it. It would be worse to pretend the discomfort wasn't there."

Drake wrote this down and looked at it for a while afterward.

---

## Chapter Five: Scales

The assassin arrived on Drake's ninth day and left without completing his mission, which Drake only found out about later.

Scales was French, professional, and had apparently taken the contract with a degree of reluctance unusual for someone of his reputation. He had done his research on Valmere before infiltrating it, which was the specific decision of someone who trusted his own assessment over a client's briefing, and what his research had told him was that the country was not what its enemies claimed it was. He took the contract anyway because he was a professional and because the reconnaissance component was genuinely low-risk and because the opportunity component — eliminate a High Council member if the chance arose — seemed unlikely enough to be theoretical.

He got past Valmere's perimeter security because he was very good and because he had studied Clive's range carefully enough to move just below it. He spent three days inside Valmere gathering intelligence. He found what Drake had found, which was a country that functioned the way it said it did, governed by people who believed in what they were doing.

He left without attempting the secondary objective.

Drake found this out from Silich, who told him in the flat, factual way that the General told him most things: that Scales had been inside, that they had monitored him without confronting him, that he had left of his own accord, and that they had chosen not to pursue him because the intelligence he was bringing back to whoever hired him was accurate and they had no objection to accurate information about Valmere reaching the world.

"You let him in and watched him," Drake said.

"We let him see what there is to see," Silich said. "That's generally more persuasive than preventing observation."

Drake was beginning to understand something about how Valmere operated that he hadn't been able to articulate before. He was still working out whether it made him more or less comfortable.

---

## Chapter Six: General Silich

The General was colder than Van Burnic by design rather than by nature, which Drake took two weeks to understand.

Silich commanded Valmere's Navy, its Air Infantry, and the Shadow Corps — an elite covert operations unit whose existence was not public and whose operations, Silich acknowledged bluntly, included involvement in disputes between other nations that had never been attributed to Valmere. Drake asked how that squared with the country's stated commitment to peaceful relations. Silich said that peace was not the same as passivity, and that some disputes were resolved by Valmere in ways that prevented larger conflicts, and that the world's ignorance of Valmere's role in those resolutions was itself a kind of gift.

"Almira doesn't know about this," Drake said.

"Almira knows what she needs to know to do her job," Silich said. "Her job is diplomacy. Mine is different."

"And Van Burnic?"

Silich looked at him with the patient expression of someone waiting for a question to resolve into something more useful. "If there is anyone who knows how Valmere became what it is," he said, "it's me. I've been with him since the beginning."

Drake heard the weight in *since the beginning* and filed it carefully.

He asked Silich what the beginning was, exactly. The General told him it was a longer conversation than they had time for today, which was the most interesting non-answer Drake had ever received.

---

## Chapter Seven: Terrad

The bombing happened on a Wednesday morning, which was not a remarkable choice of day for an unremarkable man who had made a career of being unattributed.

Terrad detonated three devices simultaneously — one near the central market, one in a transport hub, and one near the outer ring's residential district. Nobody died, which was either luck or Terrad's calculation, and Drake was never entirely sure which. There were injuries. There was damage. There was, for several hours, the specific quality of chaos that follows a sudden violent interruption of something that has been going very well.

Drake watched Van Burnic manage the crisis and understood something he hadn't been able to learn from three hours of conversation.

The leader was everywhere that morning — not performing calm, actually calm, with the specific calm of someone who has planned for bad things and is executing those plans without the friction of fear. He was on the transport hub site within twenty minutes. He spoke to residents. He spoke to his General. He did not speak to the press for four hours, and when he did he said exactly what had happened and what was known and what wasn't, which was a kind of honesty that Drake associated with people who had concluded that honesty was strategically superior to management.

They caught Terrad before nightfall. Clive found him, which was the kind of work a telepath was built for.

Drake was present for the interrogation, which Van Burnic conducted himself. This was the other side of the man — not the warmth, not the genuine interest, but the specific quality of someone who had accumulated enough power and enough patience that they could make silence do most of the heavy work. Terrad held under it for longer than Drake expected and broke under it faster than Terrad had apparently expected.

He did not reveal who sent him. He insisted, with what seemed like genuine conviction, that he had simply been unable to tolerate the existence of something so ostentatiously good, that the perfection of it made him furious in a way that no argument could address.

Van Burnic listened to this and said: "That's probably at least partly true. And someone used that truth to point you at us."

Terrad didn't say anything.

"I know who sent you," Van Burnic said. "I'm asking you to tell me because I'd like to know if you know."

Terrad said he didn't know. Van Burnic considered him for a moment and then said he believed him, which seemed to surprise Terrad more than the interrogation had.

Drake walked out of that room with two things he hadn't walked in with: a clear picture of what Van Burnic was when things went wrong, and a question about what it meant that Van Burnic already knew who sent Terrad and was not planning to say so publicly. Both of those things were worth knowing. Neither of them resolved simply.

---

## Chapter Eight: The High Council

Drake found out about the High Council on his thirty-first day, which was also the day he was supposed to file his final assessment.

He found out the way you find out things in Valmere that the country doesn't conceal so much as not volunteer — through a conversation with Silich that had started as a debrief on the Terrad incident and had become something else in the way that conversations become things when both parties have been circling a subject long enough.

The Council was a structure. It had members, meetings, votes, records. It had the appearance of distributed governance. It was not the source of Valmere's decisions, which originated with Van Burnic and always had, because Van Burnic had been planning Valmere for longer than any council member had been alive.

"How long," Drake said.

Silich looked at him with the patient expression again. "The deal with your country was made in the 1960s," he said. "Van Burnic was present for it. He was the same age he is now."

Drake sat with this.

"The council exists to ensure that Valmere looks like what Van Burnic believes it should be," Silich said. "A country governed by consensus. A country that belongs to its people. He believes this. He built it this way because he believes it. The council is real in every way except the source of its authority."

"And the source of its authority is an immortal man who decided to build a perfect country."

"Who has spent," Silich said carefully, "a very long time thinking about what a country would need to be to deserve the loyalty of the people inside it. And then built that."

Drake went back to his room and looked at the two reports he'd been writing — the one for his government and the one he hadn't sent to anyone — and understood that they needed to become one report, and that writing it was going to require him to decide something.

---

## Chapter Nine: What Drake Decided

He asked to meet with Van Burnic alone.

Van Burnic received him in the same room as before, with the same view of the waterways, and waited with the patience of someone who had been waiting for this specific conversation and had not been in a hurry about when it arrived.

Drake told him what he knew.

Van Burnic listened to all of it without interrupting.

"What do you want to know?" he said, when Drake finished.

"I want to know if any of it matters," Drake said. "Whether the truth about how this was built changes what it is."

Van Burnic was quiet for a moment. Outside, the water moved through the channels and the turbines turned and the city went about its Wednesday with the specific ease of a place that worked.

"I'm going to tell you something," Van Burnic said, "that I've told very few people. Not because it's secret — I'd tell anyone who asked directly — but because most people don't ask directly."

He told Drake what he was. Not everything — not the full history, which was longer than Drake was prepared for — but enough. Enough to understand the scope of the project, the length of the intention, the specific patience of someone who had looked at what nations tended to become and had decided to try once, carefully, to build something that didn't.

"The council is real," Van Burnic said. "The people on it make real decisions about real things. I built the structure and I set the principles and then I gave it to them, and they govern within those principles. The fact that the principles came from me doesn't make the governance false. It makes it founded."

"And if they decide something you disagree with?"

"They have," Van Burnic said. "Twice. I told them I disagreed and why. I was overruled once. I accepted it."

Drake looked at him.

"I'm not interested in controlling Valmere," Van Burnic said. "I'm interested in it working. Those are different things."

Drake filed his report to his government. It was accurate and thorough and recommended a full diplomatic relationship with Valmere on the grounds that the country was what it presented itself as being and that American interests were better served by partnership than by continued skepticism.

He did not include what he knew about Van Burnic's nature. He told himself this was because it was unverifiable. He also told himself it was because he had decided it wasn't relevant to the question the report was answering, which was whether Valmere was worth trusting.

He had decided it was.

He stayed.

---

## Epilogue: The Thirty-Fifth Day

Almira found him on the outer ring, looking at the water.

"You filed your report," she said.

"I filed a report," Drake said.

She understood the distinction. She had been a diplomat long enough to understand the distinction between what was reported and what was known, and what the gap between them meant about a person's choices.

"Van Burnic told me you asked him directly," she said.

"He tells you things."

"He tells me most things," Almira said. "Not everything. But most."

Drake looked at the waterways, at the city beyond them, at the spires of the central island catching the afternoon light in a way that looked, if you were in the right mood, genuinely like something that had been built by people who loved it.

"Is it enough?" he said. "Knowing what it's built on?"

Almira considered this with the genuine attention she gave to most things. "I think that's the wrong question," she said. "The question isn't whether the foundation is perfect. It's whether what's built on it is worth having."

She looked at the city.

"I think it is," she said. "I've been here long enough to know what it does for people. What it gives them. The foundation is what it is. The city is what I can see."

Drake thought about his government, about the secondary objective he had arrived with and not completed, about the report he had filed and the things it didn't say. He thought about the other report, the one he'd been writing for no one, which was the most honest document he had produced in a career of careful and useful dishonesty.

He was thirty-five days into what had been scheduled as a two-week visit.

He asked Almira how the citizenship process worked.

She told him it was two years, which Valmere had chosen deliberately — long enough to be meaningful, short enough to not be punitive. She told him it involved residency, a language proficiency test in English or any other Valmerie-recognized tongue, and a review by the Citizenship Council, which was a subset of the High Council and operated with full autonomy over its decisions.

He said he would like to begin.

She said she would send him the forms, and she did, and he filled them out that evening in his room with the view of the waterway, and the city outside went on being what it was.
