# Kryvstof

*2030. A Russian city under quarantine. A salvage crew sent in to fix what the briefing described as a contained situation and what the briefing was wrong about.*

---

## Prologue: The Briefing

The USS Salvage designation was a bureaucratic fiction that gave the United States government plausible deniability for the kind of operations that couldn't be filed under any existing category. Officially: a humanitarian and containment support unit, operating at the request of allied governments in crisis situations. Unofficially: the people you sent when a situation had already gone past the point where officially-designated people could handle it.

Kryvstof had gone past that point six weeks ago.

The briefing described an outbreak — a substance, biologically active, spreading through contact and referred to in the classified documentation as the black ooze, which was the kind of naming convention that suggested whoever wrote the documentation had given up on clinical distance somewhere around week three. The city was quarantined. The Russian government had withdrawn its forces from the inner zones. Civilian population: largely unaccounted for. Threat profile: elevated.

Hythm read the briefing twice and then put it down and looked at the ceiling for a while. He had his memories back. He had spent six months after New York in the specific limbo of someone who has remembered who they are and has not yet decided what to do with the knowledge, and had eventually concluded that the answer to the question of what you did when you remembered you were a king was: whatever needed doing, because someone had to.

He did not want to join the USS Salvage crew. He joined anyway.

Leon had been sent by Aluccard, which was how most things involving Leon began. Drexl Voss was there because he was always there when the situation was exactly this kind of bad. Kai was there because Elmarie had asked him to go, specifically to find Aleshin — a young martial arts master she'd sent into Kryvstof weeks before the quarantine dropped, tracking an artifact, who had gone quiet.

They landed on a Wednesday. The city was not what the briefing had described.

---

## Chapter One: The Zone

Kryvstof from the air looked like a city that had exhaled and not inhaled again.

The streets were wet and dark and the buildings were standing but wrong — not structurally wrong, just inhabited wrong, the way a space changes character when the thing living in it is different from the thing it was built for. Movement below, in the pre-dawn grey. Too much movement for a quarantined zone. Too little of it that looked like people.

They set down on the roof of a hospital that had been marked as a safe point in the briefing and found Wendall there, which was not in the briefing.

He was exactly what he looked like — a military veteran who had been in Kryvstof when the outbreak happened, who had been prepared for any situation and had found that *any* was a smaller category than he'd thought. He had survived six weeks in the zone through the specific combination of preparation and stubbornness that tends to get people through things that aren't survivable if you stop to think about them carefully. He looked at the salvage crew with the expression of a man who had stopped expecting rescue and was recalibrating in real time.

"How bad is it," Hythm said.

Wendall looked out at the city. "The briefing said elevated threat profile," he said.

"Yes."

"They undersold it," Wendall said, which proved to be the most accurate intelligence assessment they received from any source for the duration of the mission.

---

## Chapter Two: The Ooze

The black ooze in London had been a weapon — a substance in the hands of an order that understood its properties and deployed it selectively. What was in Kryvstof was the same substance in the hands of nobody, which was considerably worse.

It moved through the city like an infection that had decided to express itself as weather — not in the air, but on surfaces, under doorways, in the water that pooled in the streets. The people it had reached were still mobile, which was the specific quality that made them threatening: they moved with the purposeless momentum of something that had retained locomotion after losing everything that gave locomotion direction. Most of them were what Leon quietly called infantry — former citizens, now simply driven, the ooze having replaced whatever had been making decisions with something that had only one setting.

The military ones were worse.

Hunter zombies, Wendall had named them, in the six weeks he'd had to develop a taxonomy. Former Russian special forces, some of them still in partial kit, the ooze having preserved the muscle memory and tactical instincts while removing the restraint that had governed their use. They moved in patterns. They used cover. They were not smart but they were trained, which in the dark and the rain came close enough to the same thing to matter.

Drexl Voss moved through the first engagement with the economy of someone who had fought worse things in worse places, which he had. His sword left blue light on the wet pavement. Kai's fire was useful against the ooze directly — it burned out of infected tissue rather than spreading to it, which was information that none of them had had and that all of them immediately factored into how they moved.

Leon coordinated from the tactical position of someone who had spent years as the person who figured out what was actually happening and told everyone else. He was older now. The age had made him precise in a way that youth couldn't — not slower, just more decided, every action the result of a calculation that had already been made.

Hythm amplified his weapons with the power the crown had given back to him and led from the front, which he found was the only way he was able to lead anymore. The years of forgetting had made certain things clear: he was not someone who directed from behind.

---

## Chapter Three: Seira

They found Seira in the eastern district, which the briefing had not mentioned because the briefing had not had access to anything past the outer ring.

She had been Russian special forces — the kind that the official record listed as not existing — and she had been in Kryvstof on assignment when the outbreak happened, and the people she had been in Kryvstof with had left without her, which was the kind of thing that happened when extraction parameters were designed around acceptable losses and someone made a decision about what was acceptable.

She had been fending for herself for five weeks with the specific competence of someone who had been trained to keep fighting after the mission structure collapsed, which was the training that mattered most and that most training programs taught as a secondary skill because they couldn't quite believe the primary skill would fail. Hers had. She was still fighting.

She joined them without discussion, which was how soldiers tended to join things in the middle of situations — not with agreements or conditions but with the simple arithmetic of: more people on this side is better than fewer.

She told them about the inner district. She had gone in twice, not because she had a specific reason but because standing still felt wrong and moving toward the worst part of the situation felt more honest than away. What she had found was that the ooze was thicker there — not more dispersed but more concentrated, as if it had a source or a center, as if the outbreak had a point of origin that was still active.

"Vornakof," Wendall said, when she described what the inner district looked like.

"You know who did this," Leon said.

"I know a name," Wendall said. "Warlord. Was running operations in this region before the outbreak. Made a deal with something — I don't know what — to secure the ooze and release it. Figured he'd be immune." He paused. "He wasn't immune. But he's not gone either."

---

## Chapter Four: Plokiv

Kai found him in a basement in the inner district's residential sector, which was where Aleshin's last known position had been logged.

The basement was occupied. The occupant was infected — visibly, unmistakably, the ooze present in his tissue in a way that should have meant he was one of the infantry — and was sitting on an overturned crate reading a book by the light of a chemical lamp, which was not what infected people did.

He looked up when Kai came in. His eyes were wrong — clouded at the edges, the specific quality of someone the ooze had touched — but they focused, and tracked, and looked at Kai with the full presence of a person rather than the blank momentum of something that had been replaced.

"You're not one of them," Kai said.

"No," the man said. He had the patient delivery of someone who had had this specific conversation before and was waiting for the part where it became interesting. "My name is Plokiv. I've been infected for four weeks."

"You're still thinking."

"I'm always thinking," Plokiv said. "That's apparently the problem. Or the solution. I haven't decided which."

He told them what he'd worked out, sitting in the basement, not becoming what the ooze wanted him to become. The ooze required an absence to take hold — a gap in whatever the infected person had been using to make decisions. He still had all his gaps filled in. The ooze was present in him but could not proceed past the point where it usually found purchase, because there was nothing there for it to replace. He was, as far as he could determine, immune in the most inconvenient possible way: not by fighting it off, but by simply being too thoroughly himself for it to find a foothold.

"If that's true," Leon said quietly, "his biology is the cure."

The implications of this took approximately thirty seconds to settle.

"We need to get him out," Hythm said.

---

## Chapter Five: Aleshin

Kai found what was left of Aleshin two blocks from the basement.

He had fought — that was clear from what remained of the scene, the scorch marks on the walls that were his specific signature, the golden energy having burned out through him in ways that suggested he had done considerable damage before the end. He had gone valiant, which was the phrase that Elmarie had used when she sent Kai to find him, that he would go valiant if he went at all, and she had said it the way people say things they believe and can't let themselves dwell on.

Kai stood in the empty street in the rain and understood that he had been sent to save someone and had arrived in time to see the evidence of how the saving had failed, which was worse than arriving after the evidence was gone because the evidence was specific and the specific is always harder than the abstract.

He went back to the group. He told them. He did not say what it had cost him, because there were things you filed away in the middle of a mission and dealt with on the other side, and this was going to be one of them.

He stayed useful. That was the thing he could do. He stayed completely, totally useful for the rest of the mission, and let the grief sit in the part of him that was good at holding things until there was room for them.

---

## Chapter Six: Alexinof

They met Alexinof on the route to Vornakof's position, which was appropriate given that the route was not something you walked without someone who had already made that calculation.

He had been Russian special forces before the outbreak and had been left behind in the same sweep that left Seira — different unit, different section of the city, same decision made about acceptable losses. He had cut off his own arm when it was infected, which was the kind of decision that takes a fraction of a second and requires something in a person that most people spend their lives not having to discover whether they possess.

He was cold. Not cruel — cold, in the way that people become cold when they've been operating alone in an infected city with one arm for six weeks and have survived by removing every non-essential from their decision-making. He had no room for hesitation. He had worked out that hesitation was the thing that cost you in the zone, and had set it aside, and had survived.

He knew where Vornakof was. He had been studying Vornakof's movements for two weeks with the specific patience of someone who intended to end something and was waiting until the ending was efficient rather than merely satisfying.

He joined them with the same lack of ceremony as Seira, and for the same reason. He looked at Plokiv with a very long assessment and then said, to no one in particular, that the infected man should probably stay behind them.

Plokiv said he was going.

Alexinof looked at him again and said fine, stay close.

---

## Chapter Seven: Asai

The assassin came from above, which was the direction nobody was watching because the buildings in the inner district had been assessed as structurally compromised and therefore not traversable.

They had been assessed incorrectly.

She came down the side of an outer wall with the speed of something that had either been doing this for a long time or had been changed by something in a way that made the ordinary limits less applicable. She was moving toward Plokiv, which the crew understood immediately and too late — Kai got between her and him, which created thirty seconds of the most technically demanding fight he had encountered in the zone, and lost those thirty seconds when Drexl Voss came in from the left and she adjusted and Leon changed angles and she adjusted again and she was gone before they had established enough of a perimeter to hold her.

She had not reached Plokiv.

She came back twice more over the next four hours, each time from a different angle, each time in the moments when the crew was managing something else. The second time she got closer. The third time Hythm's amplified awareness caught her before she was in range and she withdrew into the building line and they did not see her again that night.

"Someone sent her for him specifically," Leon said.

"Someone knows he's the cure," Hythm said.

"Someone doesn't want there to be one," Kai said.

---

## Chapter Eight: Scales

They lost Plokiv on the morning of the third day.

It happened in the gap between the night perimeter and the dawn movement, in the way that things happen in gaps — not through negligence but through the plain arithmetic of a crew that had been operating for seventy-two hours in an infected city and had human needs that created human windows, and Scales was very good at windows.

He was in and out before anyone understood what had happened. Professional, clean, leaving behind only the absence of someone who had been there and wasn't anymore, and the specific quality of a scene that has been managed by someone who knew exactly what he was doing and did it without waste.

Nobody knew who had sent him. He had looked at the job, as he tended to do, and had done his own assessment, and had taken the contract. What his assessment had told him about Plokiv — whether he had understood what Plokiv was, what eliminating him meant — was a question that nobody in the salvage crew ever got the chance to ask him.

Kai sat with this for a long time, in the middle of the city, in the rain, which was a different grief from Aleshin's and the same grief, and he let himself feel both of them together because there wasn't time to feel them separately.

Leon said: we move.

They moved.

---

## Chapter Nine: Vornakof

Vornakof had retained his intelligence to an extent, which was the specific quality that made him more dangerous than everything else in the zone and less dangerous than he thought he was.

He had made a deal with Patchwork to secure and release the ooze over Kryvstof, which he had presumably done for reasons that had seemed sufficient at the time and which the ooze had subsequently made irrelevant by infecting him anyway. What remained was a warlord with the ooze running through him and enough of his mind left to still want things — not specific things, but the residue of wanting, the habit of power in a form that could no longer execute the original intention.

He held the inner district's cathedral as a stronghold, not by design but by the logic of the ooze, which seemed to pool intelligence in structures the same way water pooled in low ground. He had the hunter zombies arranged as a perimeter not because he had ordered them there but because the ooze had organized itself that way, using what remained of his tactical instincts to shape the infection's distribution.

The approach was Leon's plan, executed by Drexl Voss through the left access, Kai and Seira through the right, Alexinof through the center which nobody else had considered viable and which he had assessed as viable in the specific way of someone who had been calculating viability in this city for weeks.

Hythm took the front.

Vornakof was large with the ooze and retained enough coordination to be genuinely difficult. He was not retained enough to adapt when the plan changed around him, which plans did. Kai's fire burned through the ooze's cohesion in Vornakof's left side. Drexl's blade severed what it needed to sever. Hythm's amplified weapon found the specific point where a thing that had been using borrowed intelligence finally ran out of the intelligence to borrow.

Vornakof went down without drama, which was appropriate. Things that had lost their specific character tended to lose their specific endings as well.

---

## Chapter Ten: Patchwork

They found Patchwork in the cathedral's lower level, in what had been a records room and was now a laboratory of the kind that requires considerable time and very poor judgment to assemble.

He was smaller than the ooze's reputation for him suggested — not physically diminished, but diminished in the way that things in close proximity to something much larger than themselves tend to seem smaller. He had retreated to Kryvstof after nearly dying elsewhere to continue his research and prolong his life, which had been failing by degrees since before the outbreak. The result of his research was present in the city above them, which he regarded with the equanimity of someone who had decided a long time ago that outcomes were secondary to process.

He was dangerous in the specific way that people who understand something better than their opponents are dangerous — the toxic gas, his preferred instrument, was considerably harder to approach than anything they had fought on the way in. Alexinof, who had assessed threat profiles cold for six weeks without a right arm and had concluded that the assessment was always the same question asked differently, went around the gas rather than through it, which was the only correct approach and required considerable willingness to take a route that looked wrong.

The combination of Kai's fire displacing the gas's effective range and Leon's suppression on Patchwork's movement and Drexl's specific skill with things that needed to be ended cleanly ended it.

Patchwork went down knowing that his research was unfinished. This seemed to concern him more than the going down did.

---

## Epilogue: Out

The extraction was at dawn on the fourth day, which was a Wednesday, which was the same day of the week they had arrived.

The city behind them was still infected — the ooze didn't end when its principals did, any more than a fire ends when you remove the person who lit it. Containment was back in the hands of people with official designations and the resources to maintain a perimeter. The cure was gone. Whatever Plokiv had been, whatever his biology could have provided, was gone with him, which was the specific quality of an irreversible thing: not better or worse than other losses, just permanent in a way that other losses sometimes weren't.

Wendall got on the extraction transport and sat down and said nothing for the first twenty minutes, which was the appropriate response.

Seira sat across from him and also said nothing, which was also appropriate.

Alexinof sat by himself at the back of the transport, which was where he sat. He would leave the crew at the first available stop — not because the mission was over, but because he operated alone and had operated alone long enough that the mode felt structural rather than situational. He would be useful in the world. He would find the places where being useful was necessary and go to them without announcement.

Kai looked out the window at the city receding beneath them and thought about Aleshin's scorch marks on the wall, the specific signature of a fight that had ended without him, and thought about Plokiv in the basement reading a book by chemical light, and held both of these things the way you hold things that deserve to be held before you put them somewhere you can live with.

Leon sat beside Hythm and said: "Aluccard will want a full report."

"I know," Hythm said.

"Are you going back?"

Hythm looked at the city. Five years from now, somewhere in the mid-Atlantic, a man named Van Burnic would announce a nation to the world. The ooze would still be out there — in other cities, in other forms, in the hands of people who understood what it did and found that useful. The work was not over.

"Yes," Hythm said. "I'm going back."

He looked at the crown on his hand — not a physical crown, but the sense of it, the weight of remembering — and thought that going back was the most honest answer he had for most questions these days.

The transport climbed and the city became small and the city became a pattern of lights in the dark and the city became something you had to look carefully to distinguish from the rest of the country around it.

Scales was already somewhere else.
