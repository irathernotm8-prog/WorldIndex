# Atlas

*The story of a bodyguard who believed in something, a priest who bargained with something
worse, a detective who wasn't human to begin with, and the night the city paid for all
three of them at once.*

---

## Prologue: The Quiet Shift

Nobody in Atlas, Louisiana agreed on when it started, which was itself the first sign that
something had.

Ordinary trouble announces itself. A hurricane gets a name. A crime wave gets a headline.
What happened to Atlas in the early years of that decade didn't announce anything — it
just accumulated, the way rot accumulates in a foundation, unnoticed by everyone standing
on the floor above it. A butcher's hands shook when they used to be steady. A dockworker's
kids stopped answering to their own names for whole afternoons at a time before snapping
back like nothing had happened. Confession booths in three different parishes reported an
uptick in nobody wanting to talk about anything specific, just a general sense that the
walls of the city had gotten thinner somewhere, and something on the other side of them had
noticed.

The old-timers down on the docks by the Arkham warehouses — a stretch of the waterfront
named, nobody quite remembered why, after some scholar's town up north — said the river
had always carried more than water past Atlas. That the city sat where it sat because
something underneath had wanted company once, a long time ago, and had never quite lost the
habit of reaching up to check.

Most people didn't think about any of this. Most people had rent to make and children to
feed and jazz to listen to on a Friday night, and a city thinning out at the edges was
somebody else's problem, assuming it was anybody's problem at all.

It was about to become the specific problem of three men who had no business solving it
together, and one god who wanted them to.

## Chapter One: Muscle for a Priest

Bruce had made his living with his hands for twenty years, which is a polite way of saying
he had hurt people for money and had gotten old enough to have complicated feelings about
it.

He'd come up through the outfit the way a lot of big, quiet men came up through the outfit —
because he was good at the one thing they needed and asked no questions about the rest —
and he'd gone out the same way most of them eventually did: not with a bang, but with a
quiet, mutual understanding that his usefulness had a shelf life and the shelf life was
over. Nobody put a bullet in him. That was, by outfit standards, practically an honor. He
took the dismissal as a mercy and never went looking for the reasons behind it.

What he had left, after the outfit was done with him, was his hands, his faith, and a
reputation good enough to rent out. Bodyguard work paid worse than enforcement had and let
him sleep at night, which Bruce had decided, somewhere in his forties, was a trade he was
willing to make.

The job that found him in the spring came through a mutual acquaintance who described it,
with visible discomfort, as "the priest thing."

"He's not in trouble with anybody I know," the acquaintance said. "That's what's strange
about it. He's asking for muscle like a man who knows something's coming, not like a man
running from something that already caught him."

Bruce was a churchgoing man, had been his whole life, the one part of himself the outfit
years had never quite managed to grind down. Taking a job protecting a priest felt, for a
moment, like being handed something he hadn't known he was owed.

He had no idea, walking into that rectory for the first time, what he was actually being
hired to carry.

## Chapter Two: What the Navy Left in Thompson

Before he was Father Tom, he had been a sailor named Thompson, and before that, nothing
you'd call remarkable at all.

He'd gone to war the way most of his generation had — because it was there, because
everyone he knew was going, because the alternative required an explanation he didn't have
ready — and had come home from it with the specific quiet of a man who assumed he'd already
seen the worst the universe had on offer. The Pacific had taken friends from him in ways he
didn't discuss. He'd made his peace, as much as anyone made peace with that particular war,
and had told himself the account was closed.

Atlas disagreed.

It started as things that could still, at the time, be explained away — a light in the
water that wasn't a boat, a sound from an empty hold that wasn't the hull settling. Thompson
had a sailor's practical relationship with fear and didn't scare easily. What finally broke
that practicality was a night off the Louisiana coast he never fully described to anyone
afterward, except to say that something had come up out of the water that was not a fish
and was not a man, and had looked at him the way a man looks at something he intends to
finish eating later.

He should have died that night. By every account he ever gave, halfway or reluctant as they
were, something else had intervened — a light, a voice, a sense of a hand closing around
him that had nothing to do with the Navy or with luck. He never called it anything but
divine intervention, and never elaborated further, because the alternative was admitting he
didn't actually understand what had saved him, only that it had.

He left the Navy and, in the years after, found his way toward the collar, the way people
who've been handed a second life they don't feel they earned sometimes go looking for
somewhere to put the debt.

## Chapter Three: A Rather Un-Priestly Bargain

By the time Bruce met him, Thompson had been Father Tom for the better part of a decade,
and had accumulated, in that decade, considerably more secrets than a parish priest was
generally expected to carry.

He kept them well. He preached decent, unremarkable sermons, buried the dead, married the
living, and underneath all of it, quietly, kept a private ledger of the city's darker
business that would have gotten him defrocked twice over if the diocese had ever seen it.
Atlas's underworld — the literal one, not the metaphor — had a ruler, and Father Tom had,
several years back, made the ruler's acquaintance in a manner he would never have condoned
from the pulpit.

Dreyfus had once been a king, a very long time ago, in a kingdom whose name meant nothing to
anyone left alive. What he was now — a possessor, a landlord of the dead, a patient and
unhurried ruler of whatever lay beneath the city's foundations — didn't leave much room for
subtlety in his dealings. In this era he had no lieutenants to send in his place, no smooth
operators handling his accounts. When Dreyfus wanted a deal made, Dreyfus made it himself,
borrowing whatever body was nearest and convenient to have the conversation in.

Father Tom had gone to him — for reasons he never fully explained to Bruce, though the
shape of it involved a threat to the parish and a protection Father Tom hadn't known how
else to secure — and had come away with a flame-lit medallion he wore under his collar from
that day forward, and a promise, vaguely worded in the particular way that devils prefer
their promises worded, that he would give Dreyfus "something" in return, when the time came.

He told himself, in the years after, that he'd find a way to renegotiate before it mattered.
Every priest who has ever made a bad bargain has told himself that.

He hired Bruce not long after making the deal. He never once considered that the two
decisions were connected. That was, in its own quiet way, the worst mistake of his life.

## Chapter Four: The Butcher on Third Street

Ricky ran the best butcher shop on Arkham's Third Street, had run it for eleven years, and
had never once, in all that time, given anybody cause to look at him twice.

That changed in increments too small to report to anyone official. The scale weighed a
little differently. The cuts came out a little stranger. A regular customer swore she'd
seen something in the back freezer that wasn't beef and wasn't pork and wasn't anything she
had a polite word for, and had told herself she'd imagined it, because the alternative meant
walking three extra blocks for her Sunday roast and admitting something was wrong with the
neighborhood she'd lived in her whole life.

Father Tom heard about it the way a parish priest hears about everything eventually — in
confession, secondhand, wrapped in the deniability people extend to their own eyes when
what those eyes have seen doesn't fit anywhere comfortable. He mentioned it to Bruce mostly
in passing, the way you mention a thing you intend to look into eventually and not the thing
that turns out to matter most.

It was Chiro who took it seriously first.

The dragon-kin detective had been circling the same rumors from his own direction for
weeks — disappearances that didn't connect on paper but connected, unmistakably, on the
ground, a smell under the smell of the Third Street shop that his true nose (carefully
hidden, along with the rest of him, behind a working man's face and a tired detective's
hat) recognized as something considerably older than a butcher's trade should carry.

"Whatever's gotten into that shop," Chiro told Father Tom, the first time the three men sat
in the same room together, "didn't come from anywhere human."

## Chapter Five: Three Men Who Weren't Quite Any One Thing

Chiro's kind had once walked Atlas openly, back when the city had a different name and a
different relationship with what lived under it. That had been a long time before the war,
before the outfit, before any of the streets Bruce had grown up hurting people on had even
been paved. What remained of the dragon-kin now numbered few enough that Chiro had learned,
young, to fold his true shape down into something that could pass a human checkpoint
without incident — a working suit, a detective's badge from an agency that mostly left him
alone because he closed more cases than anyone else on the payroll, and a private, running
habit of asking himself, in difficult moments, what the great Draven would have done.

He never fully explained who Draven was to either of his new companions. It wasn't
secrecy, exactly. It was the specific, private grief of a species with almost no elders
left to explain its own history to itself, holding onto the one name that still meant
something the way a smaller family holds onto a photograph.

The three of them made an unlikely trio by any measure — a lapsed enforcer with a rosary in
his coat pocket, a priest with a debt he hadn't told either of the others about, and a
detective who had to remember, consciously, every single day, not to let his true face show
through at the wrong moment. What held them together wasn't friendship, not yet. It was the
specific, practical fact that whatever was thinning out Atlas's walls needed more than one
kind of expertise to even properly see, let alone stop.

Bruce trusted Father Tom because Father Tom wore a collar and Bruce had never learned how
not to trust that. Father Tom trusted Chiro because Chiro's information was always good.
Chiro trusted the other two exactly as far as their usefulness extended, and was privately,
quietly surprised at how far that turned out to be.

## Chapter Six: What Wore the Kraken's Face

They found Cthulhu in the flooded sub-level under the Third Street shop, and for a long,
terrible moment, all three of them believed they had found their answer.

He did not, at first glance, contradict the impression. Something that had once been called
the Kraken in an older, more mythological century — a vast sea-thing that had fed on faith
before faith had names, that had outlasted Poseidon's whole pantheon and simply picked up a
new name and a new coastline when the old world stopped being interesting — now walked
Atlas's underside in a shape that could pass, at a distance and in poor light, for a very
tall man in an overcoat. He used the people of the city the way a bored, ancient intelligence
uses whatever is nearest to hand: not out of hatred, but out of a curiosity that had never
once considered that its subjects might object.

Chiro's rumors, Ricky's shop, the disappearances — Cthulhu did not deny involvement in any
of it, exactly. He simply found the accusation beneath him.

"I have taken things from this city," he told them, with the mild, unbothered honesty of
something too old to bother lying. "I have not taken your twins."

That was the first time any of the three men had heard the word used in connection with
what was happening, and Cthulhu, watching their faces, allowed himself something almost
like amusement.

"You'll want to look elsewhere for that," he said. "I only experiment. What's happening to
those two children is being fed on. There's a considerable difference, and I'd thank you to
recognize it before you waste what time you have left correcting the wrong mistake."

He returned to the water without further explanation, the way something that old returns to
anything: unhurried, and entirely certain the conversation would still be there to resume
whenever he next found it interesting.

## Chapter Seven: The Guidry Twins

The Guidry twins had been, by every account from before, unremarkable children — eleven
years old, inseparable, the kind of quiet that comes from having had, since birth, exactly
one other person who never needed anything explained to them twice.

What Bruce, Father Tom, and Chiro found in the Guidry house was not, strictly, either child
anymore. Something had gotten into the space between them — the specific, private
understanding twins are supposed to share only with each other — and had used that space
the way water uses a crack, filling it, widening it, wearing the children's faces from the
inside while something patient and enormous fed on whatever they'd had left to lose.

Father Tom recognized the signature of it before either of the other two did, because
recognizing corrupted faith was, in a bitter way, his actual trade.

"This isn't a demon feeding on a body," he said quietly, watching two identical faces turn
toward him with an expression neither child had ever worn in life. "This is something
feeding on suffering itself. The children are just where it's decided to keep the tap
running."

Chiro's rumors had a name for that kind of hunger, one his own people had old, cautious
stories about, back when there had still been enough dragon-kin left to keep stories at all.

"Nyarlathotep," he said, and the word sat in the room afterward like something that had been
waiting, patiently, for someone to finally say it out loud.

---

## Chapter Eight: The Root of It

Nyarlathotep did not hide, once named. It had never needed to.

It walked Atlas in a shape that dressed like a businessman and spoke like one too, if a
businessman's product had ever been despair rather than anything you could put a price tag
on. It was, the three of them came to understand in the worst possible way, the actual root
of everything that had gone quietly wrong in the city for longer than any of them had been
paying attention — the butcher's shaking hands, the thinning walls, the general and
specific suffering of a city it had selected the way a farmer selects good soil, not out of
malice exactly, but out of an appetite that simply required somewhere fertile to feed.

It did not fear Bruce's faith, or Father Tom's medallion, or the true shape hiding behind
Chiro's borrowed face. It found all three, if anything, additional flavor.

Their first attempt to confront it directly went as badly as three mortal men should have
expected a confrontation with an outer god's herald to go. Bruce's fists found nothing solid
to land on. Father Tom's medallion burned hot against his chest and did not so much protect
him as buy him the specific, humiliating mercy of being allowed to retreat. Chiro's true
form flickered through for one unguarded second — enough for Nyarlathotep to look at him
with open, delighted recognition, the way a collector looks at something rarer than he'd
expected to find in this particular gutter.

They survived only because Nyarlathotep let them. It had all the time in creation. It could
afford to be patient about finishing them, the same way it had been patient about the twins,
the same way it had been patient about the whole slow, quiet unraveling of a city it had
decided, at some point nobody could name, belonged to it.

They left that meeting with one shared, terrible understanding: nothing any of the three of
them had was going to be enough.

## Chapter Nine: A Name Older Than Fear

It was Father Tom, in the end, who found the only name left to try — buried in the same
private ledger of the city's darker business that had gotten him into his bargain with
Dreyfus in the first place, a name older and colder than anything Nyarlathotep answered to.

Yog-Sothoth did not fight things. It did not have enemies in any sense a mortal would
recognize the word. It simply was, at every point in every place, indifferent to all of it
in a way that made even Nyarlathotep's appetite look almost intimate by comparison. Summoning
it wasn't a plan. It was a surrender dressed up as one — an admission that nothing the three
of them possessed could touch the actual problem, and that the only thing left to try was
something too large to care about the problem's size at all.

Chiro argued against it first, and hardest, invoking the great Draven's memory as the
closest thing he had to scripture on the subject: some fires were not meant to be answered
with bigger fires. Father Tom, who had already made one deal he didn't fully understand the
terms of, argued that a city being fed on slowly was no better than a city ended quickly,
and that at least the second option came with the mercy of being over.

Bruce said the least of the three of them and meant the most by it. He had spent his whole
adult life being the muscle other people hired to solve problems they didn't want to
solve themselves. He understood, better than either of his more educated companions, exactly
what it meant to be the thing thrown at a problem too large for anyone throwing it to fully
account for the cost.

"Do it," he said. "Whatever's left after, I'll carry it."

He had no idea, saying it, how literally that promise was about to be collected.

---

## Chapter Ten: What Yog-Sothoth Left Behind

The summoning worked, in the specific and terrible sense that it stopped Nyarlathotep. It
did not work in any sense that felt, afterward, like a victory.

Yog-Sothoth did not fight the herald so much as note it, the way an accountant notes an
error, and correct it by the simplest method available: it took the city itself, and
everything inside the city that had drawn its attention — Nyarlathotep, Cthulhu, whatever
was left of the Guidry twins, Polygaster's slow black seed still working somewhere in the
deep water below Third Street, all of it, indiscriminately — and removed Atlas from
existence, the way a hand removes a splinter, without malice and entirely without concern
for what had been living in the wound.

It carried what it had gathered toward the far, uncaring reaches of space, because that was
where it had decided such things belonged, and it left Bruce, Father Tom, and Chiro adrift
in the specific, silent nowhere a city leaves behind when a god has decided the city was
never truly the point.

There was no ground under them. There was no city to go home to, because the city was gone,
folded into whatever Yog-Sothoth had actually wanted with it, which none of the three of
them would ever fully understand.

There was only the three of them, and the quiet, and the sense — the same sense that had
made Bruce's faith feel, once, like the one solid thing he owned — that something else was
still coming to collect.

## Epilogue: The Devil's Due

Dreyfus found them in the nowhere easily enough. Nowhere had never troubled him. He had
ruled a version of it for longer than Atlas had existed at all.

He did not gloat. That was, in its own way, the worst part — he simply appeared, in a
borrowed shape that flickered at the edges the way all his borrowed shapes did, and reminded
Father Tom, gently and without malice, of an old promise made years before in exchange for a
medallion that had, by any honest accounting, done exactly what it was bought to do.

"You promised me something," Dreyfus said. "I've come to collect it."

Father Tom had spent years telling himself he'd find a way to renegotiate before it
mattered. He had never found the way. He hadn't even, in the end, understood what he'd
actually promised, which was the specific cruelty of the bargains Dreyfus preferred: not a
lie, exactly, just a truth left vague enough that a desperate man would fill in the kindest
possible meaning himself, and be wrong.

The something had always been Bruce.

Dreyfus took hold of him the way he took hold of anything he wanted to speak through — not
asking, because asking had never been necessary — and where Bruce had been, something else
began, slowly and irreversibly, to stand up in his place: a demon soul collector, one of
what would come to be called, in cities and centuries neither Bruce nor anyone standing in
that nowhere could have imagined, the Minodas. In this era, in this deal, there was only
one of them, made personally, by a king still doing his own collecting because he hadn't yet
built the network of dealers that would someday make Bruce's transformation into a common
enough occurrence to need a name for the whole category.

What was left of Bruce, in the last human seconds of it, understood exactly what had been
paid, and by whom, and for what. He had given his faith, his years, his hands, to protecting
a man who had, without ever meaning to, sold him to settle a debt Bruce had never agreed to
and never even known existed.

He killed Father Tom in the same breath he stopped being entirely Bruce. It was not
calculated. It was not anything either of them would have called justice. It was simply
what was left of a good man's rage, discovering, in the last instant it belonged to him,
exactly how thoroughly his own goodness had been spent without his permission.

Chiro was the only one left, in the end, standing in a nowhere that had once been a city, a
dragon-kin detective very far from anyone who remembered what his true face looked like,
watching a debt collector wearing his friend's ruined features turn, slowly, to consider
what was owed next.

Somewhere, in a version of the world that still had a shape to it, a name would eventually
attach itself to what Bruce had become — a beastly businessman, a dealer in devil's bargains,
polite and patient and utterly without mercy, offering deals to anyone foolish enough to
take them. Nobody who met him then would ever learn he'd once had a rosary in his coat
pocket, or a reputation good enough to rent out, or a city called Atlas that he'd tried, in
his own quiet and unglamorous way, to protect.

The city was gone. The debt was paid. And somewhere far beyond both of those facts, still
drifting where Yog-Sothoth had left it, the thing that had once been an honest man's faith
went on doing, forever, the only job it had left.
